# Building an emoji-based emotional check-in tool for Christian addiction recovery

**A 20–30 item custom emoji check-in tool is both clinically supported and practically achievable.** Research demonstrates that daily emotion identification reduces relapse risk by **27% per unit increase in emotion differentiation**, that emoji/icon-based interfaces achieve **81–92% compliance rates** in addiction populations, and that Parrott's emotion taxonomy provides an ideal structural backbone with exactly 25 secondary emotions matching the target range. The critical innovation required is supplementing this secular framework with approximately 5–8 spiritual and recovery-specific states — conviction, surrender, craving, serenity, spiritual longing, and others — that standard taxonomies systematically omit but that are essential in Christian recovery contexts. What follows is a detailed synthesis of the research across clinical tools, visual design science, emotion taxonomy structure, faith-based emotional categories, and EMA best practices.

---

## Existing custom-icon emotion tools reveal a clear design spectrum

The landscape of custom-designed (non-Unicode) emotion tools in clinical and therapeutic settings spans from minimal 5-face scales to comprehensive 144-word systems, with the most successful tools clustering around **5–8 primary categories that expand into 20–30 subcategories**.

**Daylio**, the most downloaded mood-tracking app globally (50M+ downloads), uses **5 proprietary circular face icons** (Rad, Good, Meh, Bad, Awful) with color coding from teal to dark purple. Its success validates the simplest possible entry point — a single tap on a face. Users can expand into unlimited custom sub-emotions under each category, effectively creating a layered 20+ emotion system. **How We Feel**, developed by the Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence and named one of Apple's top apps of 2022, takes the opposite approach: **144 emotion words** arranged on a 2D color-coded grid (Energy × Pleasantness) across four quadrants — red (high energy, unpleasant), yellow (high energy, pleasant), blue (low energy, unpleasant), and green (low energy, pleasant). Rather than face icons, it uses abstract color positioning, proving that non-face visual systems can also succeed.

Among children's programs, **Kimochis** offers the closest analog to a comprehensive emoji set: **29 named plush feeling pillows** with embroidered facial expressions covering emotions from "brave" and "curious" to "insecure" and "jealous." **LEGO Build Me Emotions** uses **8 double-sided yellow face bricks** (16 distinct expressions) with simple line-art eyes and mouths. The **Boardmaker Picture Communication Symbols (PCS)** library provides **8 to 120 emotion symbols** depending on the set, specifically designed for maximum recognition by individuals with communication disorders — the clinical gold standard for icon clarity.

In clinical assessment, four tools stand out. The **Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale** (1983) uses **6 hand-drawn faces** from smiling to crying, deliberately kept black-and-white (the Foundation explicitly prohibits adding color). The **Visual Analog Mood Scales (VAMS)** provides **7 schematic faces** for distinct mood states (sad, afraid, angry, tired, energetic, happy, confused), validated with a "No-Word" version proving faces alone convey meaning — critical for populations with language barriers. The **Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM)** by Bradley and Lang (1994) uses **three sets of 5 full-body cartoon manikins** measuring valence, arousal, and dominance, demonstrating that body posture can capture emotional dimensions that faces alone cannot. These tools' color-coding patterns are remarkably consistent across systems: **red = negative high-energy, blue = negative low-energy, green = positive calm, yellow = positive high-energy**.

## How to make each icon unambiguously represent one emotion

The visual design literature, anchored in Paul Ekman's Facial Action Coding System (FACS), identifies clear principles for translating emotions into simplified iconography. FACS catalogs 44 Action Units (AUs) — individual muscle movements — whose combinations produce recognizable emotional expressions. For custom icon design, three facial features carry nearly all the differentiating information.

**Eyebrows are the single most critical differentiator.** Raised brows (AU1+AU2) signal surprise or fear. Inner brows raised with outer brows lowered (AU1+AU4) signal sadness. Lowered V-shaped brows (AU4) signal anger. An asymmetric brow (one raised) signals skepticism or confusion. **Mouth shape is the second most critical feature:** upturned corners for happiness, downturned for sadness, O-shaped for surprise, horizontally stretched for fear, tight thin line for anger, raised upper lip with nose wrinkle for disgust, and one-sided raise for contempt. **Eye shape provides the third differentiating layer:** wide-open for surprise and fear, narrowed from below for anger and suspicion, squinted from above (Duchenne markers) for genuine happiness, and drooping for sadness or fatigue.

Franco and Fugate's landmark 2021 study in *Frontiers in Psychology* adapted FACS to systematically code 31 emoji faces across 6 platform versions, finding that **the same Unicode character can trigger different emotion categorizations** depending on how platforms render the underlying Action Units. This finding alone justifies using custom-designed icons rather than standard emoji for any clinical tool.

**The hardest emotion pairs to distinguish in simplified icons** are fear versus surprise (sharing raised brows and wide eyes — differentiated primarily by mouth shape and sweat drops), contempt versus disgust (contempt requires an asymmetric mouth, which is subtle in minimal art), and the shame-sadness-guilt cluster (all involving downturned features, requiring body posture or gaze direction to disambiguate). Research by Miller et al. (2016) found **25% sentiment disagreement** even among users of the same platform looking at identical emoji, with negative emotions showing the highest ambiguity.

The practical design recommendations that emerge from this literature are:

- **Always pair icons with text labels** — the Nielsen Norman Group and clinical validation studies consistently show this is the single most impactful intervention for accuracy
- **Exaggerate the one distinguishing feature** that separates confusable emotions (e.g., horizontal mouth + sweat for fear vs. round open mouth for surprise)
- **Use multiple redundant cues** — combine eyebrow shape + eye shape + mouth shape + one supplementary marker (tears, sweat drops, blush, steam)
- **Use color as secondary reinforcement only**, never as the sole differentiator — Jonauskaite et al.'s 2020 study across 30+ countries with 15,000+ participants confirmed strong color-emotion associations (red-anger at 73%, yellow-joy at 90%) but low specificity, and colorblind accessibility demands shape-based differentiation
- **Maintain stylized abstraction** rather than photorealism — more abstract faces process faster cognitively, avoid cultural specificity, and allow greater exaggeration of distinguishing features
- **Minimum touch target of 44×44 pixels** per WCAG 2.2, with SVG format for scalability

## Parrott's taxonomy provides the ideal structural backbone

W. Parrott's (2001) three-tier emotion classification — originally derived from Shaver et al.'s (1987) empirical cluster analysis — organizes approximately **135 emotion words into 6 primary categories, 25 secondary emotions, and numerous tertiary distinctions**. The 25 secondary emotions land squarely within the 20–30 target range, making this framework uniquely suited as the structural backbone.

The six primary categories are **Love** (with secondaries: Affection, Lust, Longing), **Joy** (Cheerfulness, Zest, Contentment, Pride, Optimism, Enthrallment, Relief), **Surprise** (Surprise/Amazement), **Anger** (Irritation, Exasperation, Rage, Disgust, Envy, Torment), **Sadness** (Suffering, Sadness, Disappointment, Shame, Neglect, Sympathy), and **Fear** (Horror, Nervousness). Each secondary emotion contains 1–17 tertiary emotions providing fine-grained vocabulary — for example, "Cheerfulness" encompasses amusement, bliss, glee, delight, euphoria, and ecstasy.

Compared to alternatives, Parrott's framework wins on multiple criteria. The **Willcox Feeling Wheel** (1982) offers 72 feelings across three concentric rings with different primaries (Mad, Sad, Scared, Joyful, Powerful, Peaceful) and is excellent for therapeutic conversation but has **36 middle-ring emotions** — slightly too many for a daily check-in tool. The **Geneva Emotion Wheel** provides 20 emotion families already adapted into a mobile app (GEWMI), but lacks hierarchical grouping. **Plutchik's wheel** has only 8 primaries and relies on abstract dyad combinations (love = joy + trust) that are conceptually difficult to map to discrete icons.

Parrott's framework has also proven computational compatibility. The SuperEmotion dataset (2025) maps 519,812 NLP samples to Parrott/Shaver's primary classes. Multiple tweet classification studies achieve **>90% accuracy for joy/sadness** using Parrott's hierarchy. This computational track record matters for any future data analysis of check-in patterns.

**Not all 25 secondaries translate equally well to icons.** Cheerfulness, Rage, Horror, Nervousness, Disgust, Pride, Contentment, Relief, and Surprise are straightforward. Longing, Enthrallment, Torment, Neglect, and Sympathy are harder — Enthrallment overlaps visually with Zest, Torment overlaps with Suffering, and Neglect is an unusually broad category spanning isolation, embarrassment, and humiliation. The recommended modifications for a check-in tool: merge Enthrallment into Zest, merge Torment into Rage, split Neglect into "Loneliness" and "Embarrassment," and add a "Neutral/Calm" state (absent from Parrott but present in virtually every digital mood tool). This yields approximately 24 base items before adding spiritual and recovery-specific states.

## The emotions that secular frameworks systematically miss

Standard taxonomies omit two entire domains of human emotional experience that are indispensable for Christian addiction recovery: the **vertical (God-ward) dimension** of spiritual emotions, and **addiction-specific experiential states** tied to the recovery process. These gaps are not minor oversights — they represent the core emotional terrain that Christian recovery counseling navigates daily.

**Spiritual emotions missing from secular frameworks** include **conviction** (Holy Spirit–mediated awareness of sin that draws the person *toward* God, fundamentally distinct from guilt's self-focused paralysis), **godly sorrow versus worldly sorrow** (2 Corinthians 7:10 — the former produces repentance and transformation while the latter produces despair; David's Psalm 51 contrasted with Judas's self-destruction), **worship-awe** (voluntary, relational reverence toward God, distinct from Keltner and Haidt's involuntary secular awe at vastness), **spiritual peace/shalom** (Philippians 4:7's "peace that passes understanding," an active state of right-relationship that persists amid suffering, unlike mere calm), **spiritual longing** (Psalm 42's "as the deer pants for water," existential thirst for the divine that is paradoxically both painful and sanctifying), and **acedia/spiritual dryness/dark night of the soul** (the ancient monastic term for soul-weariness and perceived divine absence, critically distinct from clinical depression in that it primarily affects the spiritual life while general functioning may remain intact, as documented with Mother Teresa).

The **Ignatian framework of consolation versus desolation** offers a particularly useful lens: consolation is not happiness but orientation toward God (one can grieve in consolation if the grief draws one Godward), while desolation is movement away from God (a spiritual "high" can be desolation if it leads away from God's will). This directional quality — emotions as *movement toward or away from God* — has no analog in secular frameworks.

**Addiction-specific states absent from standard taxonomies** include **craving** (the intense, body-mind compulsive desire to use, neurobiologically distinct from ordinary desire), **powerlessness** (Step 1's adaptive admission of lost control, paradoxically different from learned helplessness because it opens the door to help), **serenity** (the Serenity Prayer's specific quality of peace combined with acceptance, courage, and wisdom — active, not passive), **resentment** (the AA Big Book's "number one offender," chronic re-experienced bitterness that creates internal tension leading to relapse), **pink cloud euphoria** (temporary early-recovery elation that creates dangerous false security before a predictable crash at 3–4 weeks), and the **HALT states** (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired — vulnerability conditions that increase relapse risk, mapped directly to Marlatt and Gordon's relapse prevention model). The triad of **restlessness, irritability, and discontent (RID)** from the AA Big Book's "Doctor's Opinion" describes the baseline emotional state of the unrecovered person — a persistent, low-grade discomfort whose opposite is being "happy, joyous, and free."

**Key overlaps between Christian and recovery frameworks** create natural integration points: surrender to God's will and Step 3's turning over of will are nearly identical; conviction (Christian) and the guilt-shame distinction (Brown/recovery) share the same adaptive-versus-destructive structure; shalom and serenity describe the same destination from different starting points. **Celebrate Recovery**, active in 35,000+ churches across 70+ countries with 7+ million participants, already integrates these frameworks through Christ-centered 12 steps based on the Beatitudes.

For visual representation, spiritual and recovery states pose unique challenges because **faces alone are insufficient**. Conviction, surrender, worship-awe, spiritual longing, craving, and powerlessness require body posture, symbolic elements, or contextual imagery. The most promising approach draws on Orthodox/Catholic iconographic traditions (which use color symbolism, body posture, and spatial orientation to convey spiritual states) combined with modern Christian visual motifs: kneeling figures reaching toward light for spiritual longing, open upturned palms for surrender, broken chains for freedom from addiction, a dove for spiritual peace, and desert imagery for spiritual dryness.

## Evidence-based design for daily recovery check-ins

The clinical evidence strongly supports emoji-based daily emotion monitoring as both therapeutic in itself and predictive of relapse risk. The landmark study by **Anand et al. (2017)** in *Drug and Alcohol Dependence* found that among 213 substance users entering residential treatment, **every unit increase in emotion differentiation produced a 27% reduction in the likelihood of initial lapse on any given day** (OR=0.73, 95% CI [0.56, 0.95]). Lisa Feldman Barrett's broader research on emotional granularity shows that people who make fine-grained distinctions between negative feelings are **30% more flexible in regulating emotions**, less likely to drink excessively when stressed, and less likely to retaliate aggressively. Regular emotion identification practice is not merely monitoring — it is itself a therapeutic intervention that builds the very skill most impaired in addiction populations.

**Alexithymia** — difficulty identifying and describing emotions — is prevalent among individuals with substance use disorders and predicts poorer treatment outcomes, more relapse episodes, and higher rates of premature treatment discontinuation. Visual/emoji-based check-in tools directly address this deficit by providing a vocabulary bridge: users can identify an icon that "looks like how I feel" even when they lack the words, gradually building emotional vocabulary through repeated practice.

**Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) compliance rates in addiction populations are encouragingly high.** Scott, Dennis, and Gustafson (2018) achieved **81% compliance** with 5 daily assessments over 6 months. A 2025 Daily Progress System feasibility study achieved **91.9% compliance** with 3–4 daily assessments over 14 days among justice-involved participants. The Wrzus and Neubauer 2023 meta-analysis (k=477, N=677,536) found average compliance of **79%** and — critically — that the number of assessments per day did not significantly predict compliance or dropout, suggesting simplicity of each assessment matters more than frequency. The "Emoji Game" study (2025, *Psychological Assessment*) found that embedding emoji into EMA surveys increased adolescent psychiatric compliance from approximately 40% to **63%**.

The optimal check-in architecture, synthesized from this evidence, follows a **layered progressive model**:

- **Layer 1 (required, <30 seconds):** Tap one of 5–6 primary mood faces — this mirrors Daylio's proven model and creates the minimum viable data point
- **Layer 2 (encouraged, +30 seconds):** Select a more specific secondary emotion from 3–5 options under the chosen primary — this builds emotional granularity and maps to Parrott's secondary emotions
- **Layer 3 (optional, +60 seconds):** Rate intensity on a 0–10 slider, check a separate craving scale, and select contextual tags for triggers, activities, and people
- **Layer 4 (optional, +60 seconds):** Respond to a structured journal prompt (e.g., "What triggered this feeling?" or "What coping strategy will you use?")

**Timing should follow the morning pledge + evening reflection model** used by I Am Sober and supported by clinical protocols. Morning check-ins set intention and baseline mood; evening check-ins capture the day's emotional arc, triggers, and coping responses. Each check-in should take **under 2 minutes** at baseline (expandable for users who want depth). Data should be stored locally by default with opt-in sharing to counselors or sponsors — privacy is paramount in recovery contexts.

## Conclusion: a proposed 26-item framework

The research converges on a clear architecture. Use **Parrott's 25 secondary emotions as the data model**, modified by merging Enthrallment into Zest and Torment into Rage, splitting Neglect into Loneliness and Embarrassment, and adding Neutral/Calm — yielding 24 base items grouped under 6 color-coded primary headers. Supplement with **5–8 custom spiritual and recovery categories**: Conviction, Surrender, Spiritual Longing, Worship/Awe, Serenity, Craving, and Resentment — states that are emotionally distinct, clinically critical, and representable through body-posture-enhanced custom icons. This produces approximately **26–31 items** in the target range.

Each icon should be a **stylized, abstract face-and-body illustration** (not photorealistic) with exaggerated distinguishing features, accompanied by a text label, optionally color-coded by primary category, and designed to work in grayscale for accessibility. Spiritual states should incorporate symbolic elements drawn from Christian visual tradition — light, dove, cross, broken chains, desert, water, raised hands — to convey what faces alone cannot. The tool should use progressive disclosure: show 6 primary categories first, then reveal secondary/supplementary options, building users' emotional vocabulary over time while keeping daily check-ins under 2 minutes. The evidence is unambiguous that this approach — visual, layered, brief, repeated — directly builds the emotion differentiation skill that reduces relapse risk, addresses the alexithymia prevalent in addiction, and creates clinically actionable data for counselors navigating the unique emotional terrain of Christian recovery.
