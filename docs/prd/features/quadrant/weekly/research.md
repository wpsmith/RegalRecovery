# Recovery Quadrant -- Research Document

**Date:** 2026-04-23
**Purpose:** Research quadrant-based frameworks in addiction recovery to inform PRD and implementation plan for the Recovery Quadrant feature in Regal Recovery.
**Complexity Tier:** Complex (4 framework investigations, clinical evidence review, digital implementation analysis, Christian integration mapping)

---

## Executive Summary

Four major quadrant-based frameworks offer holistic models for measuring recovery wellness across multiple life dimensions: (1) Stephen Covey's Whole Person Paradigm (Body, Mind, Heart, Spirit), (2) Ken Wilber's Integral Theory AQAL (I, IT, WE, ITS), (3) SAMHSA's Four Dimensions of Recovery (Health, Home, Purpose, Community), and (4) the Biopsychosocial-Spiritual model (Biological, Psychological, Social, Spiritual). After comparing all four on actionability, clinical grounding, digital fitness, and Christian integration potential, this document recommends a **hybrid model** rooted in SAMHSA's four dimensions with Covey's Body/Mind/Heart/Spirit labels and explicit Christian integration via Mark 12:30. This hybrid gives us clinical credibility (SAMHSA), intuitive labeling (Covey), and scriptural resonance (Mark 12:30's "heart, soul, mind, strength"). The quadrant model complements the existing Life Balance Index (LBI/PCI) feature -- where LBI tracks 10 granular behavioral dimensions daily, the Recovery Quadrant provides a higher-altitude weekly self-assessment of four broad life domains, enabling pattern recognition at a different level of abstraction.

## 1. Quadrant Frameworks

### 1.1 Stephen Covey's Whole Person Paradigm

**Source:** Stephen R. Covey, *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989) and *The 8th Habit* (2004).

[Source Type: Published Book | Stephen R. Covey | 1989/2004 | ISBN 978-0743269513 / 978-0684846651 | High]

**Overview:** Covey's Habit 7 ("Sharpen the Saw") describes four dimensions of personal renewal that must be continuously maintained for sustained effectiveness:

| Dimension | Focus | Renewal Activities |
|---|---|---|
| **Body (Physical)** | Health, nutrition, exercise, rest, stress management | Exercise, balanced diet, adequate sleep, preventive healthcare |
| **Mind (Mental)** | Learning, reading, visualization, planning, writing | Reading, journaling, education, skill development, creative work |
| **Heart (Social/Emotional)** | Relationships, empathy, service, emotional intelligence | Deep connection, service to others, empathic listening, emotional self-awareness |
| **Spirit (Spiritual)** | Values, meaning, purpose, connection to something greater | Meditation, prayer, scripture study, nature immersion, value clarification |

**Key principle:** Covey emphasizes that neglecting any single dimension eventually degrades all four. The dimensions form an interdependent system -- physical exhaustion impairs mental clarity, which strains relationships, which erodes spiritual grounding. [INFERENCE | High Confidence -- directly derived from Covey's published framework description]

**Application to recovery:** Covey's model is not addiction-specific, but its emphasis on balanced self-care maps naturally to recovery. The "Sharpen the Saw" metaphor resonates with recovery maintenance -- you cannot keep working your recovery program if your foundational wellness is eroding. The framework's simplicity (only 4 categories) makes it accessible for daily self-assessment without the cognitive overhead of 10-12 dimensions. [INFERENCE | Medium Confidence]

**Strengths:**
- Intuitive, memorable labeling (Body, Mind, Heart, Spirit)
- Widely known -- many recovery participants have encountered this framework in personal development contexts
- Strong alignment with Christian anthropology (body, soul, mind, spirit)
- Simple enough for weekly self-assessment

**Limitations:**
- Not clinically validated for addiction recovery
- No built-in scoring or risk-level framework
- "Heart" conflates social/relational and emotional dimensions
- No explicit community/institutional dimension

### 1.2 Ken Wilber's Integral Theory (AQAL)

**Source:** Ken Wilber, *A Theory of Everything* (2000), *Integral Psychology* (2000), *Sex, Ecology, Spirituality* (1995).

[Source Type: Published Books | Ken Wilber | 1995-2000 | Multiple ISBNs | Medium-High]
[Source Type: Encyclopedia | Wikipedia - Integral Theory | current | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_theory_(Ken_Wilber) | Medium-High]

**Overview:** Wilber's AQAL ("All Quadrants, All Levels") model maps reality across two intersecting axes -- Individual/Collective and Interior/Exterior -- creating four quadrants:

| Quadrant | Label | Focus | Recovery Mapping |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Upper-Left (I)** | Individual Interior | Subjective experience, consciousness, emotions, identity | Self-awareness, emotional regulation, trauma processing, identity in Christ |
| **Upper-Right (IT)** | Individual Exterior | Observable behavior, biology, neurology, physical health | Sobriety behaviors, physical health, brain chemistry, sleep, exercise |
| **Lower-Left (WE)** | Collective Interior | Shared meaning, culture, values, relational bonds | Fellowship, accountability relationships, shared recovery values, faith community |
| **Lower-Right (ITS)** | Collective Exterior | Systems, institutions, social structures | Treatment programs, 12-step groups, church structures, support systems |

**Application to recovery:** John Dupuy's *Integral Recovery* (2013, USA Best Book Award winner) explicitly applies Wilber's framework to addiction treatment. The Integral Recovery program uses four practices mapped to the quadrants: "Waking Up" (spiritual/interior), "Growing Up" (psychological development), "Cleaning Up" (shadow work, emotional processing), and "Showing Up" (behavioral commitment, community engagement). [FACT | Medium Confidence]

[Source Type: Website | Integral Recovery | current | https://integralrecovery.com/ | Medium]

**Strengths:**
- Explicitly accounts for both individual and collective/community dimensions
- Recognizes that interior (subjective) and exterior (objective/behavioral) work are both necessary
- Has been applied to addiction recovery by at least one established program
- Philosophically rigorous

**Limitations:**
- Labeling is abstract and academic (I, IT, WE, ITS is not intuitive for most users)
- Requires significant explanation before users understand what each quadrant means
- Not widely known in mainstream recovery communities (SA, Celebrate Recovery)
- The collective quadrants (WE, ITS) are hard to self-assess -- they measure shared culture and systems, not individual wellness
- No established scoring methodology for self-assessment
- May feel overly philosophical for a practical daily recovery tool

### 1.3 SAMHSA's Four Dimensions of Recovery

**Source:** SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration), "SAMHSA's Working Definition of Recovery" (2012).

[Source Type: Government Agency | SAMHSA | 2012 | PEP12-RECDEF | High]

**Overview:** In 2012, SAMHSA defined recovery as "a process of change through which individuals improve their health and wellness, live a self-directed life, and strive to reach their full potential." The definition identifies **four major dimensions that support a life in recovery:**

| Dimension | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Health** | Overcoming or managing one's disease(s) or symptoms and making informed, healthy choices that support physical and emotional well-being | Abstinence, managing withdrawal, nutrition, exercise, sleep, mental health care |
| **Home** | Having a stable and safe place to live that supports recovery | Safe living environment, housing stability, home as a structured routine space |
| **Purpose** | Conducting meaningful daily activities (such as a job, school, volunteerism, family caretaking, or creative endeavors) and having the independence, income, and resources to participate in society | Employment, education, service, creative expression, daily structure |
| **Community** | Having relationships and social networks that provide support, friendship, love, and hope | Recovery fellowship, family relationships, faith community, accountability partners, social connection |

**SAMHSA also defined 10 Guiding Principles of Recovery:**
1. Recovery emerges from hope
2. Recovery is person-driven
3. Recovery occurs via many pathways
4. Recovery is holistic
5. Recovery is supported by peers and allies
6. Recovery is supported through relationship and social networks
7. Recovery is culturally-based and influenced
8. Recovery is supported by addressing trauma
9. Recovery involves individual, family, and community strengths and responsibility
10. Recovery is based on respect

[FACT | High Confidence -- SAMHSA's definition is the U.S. federal government's official position on recovery, widely cited in clinical literature]

**Strengths:**
- Clinically authoritative -- the U.S. government's official framework for recovery
- Widely adopted in substance abuse treatment programs
- Explicitly designed for addiction recovery (not adapted from another domain)
- Covers both individual wellness and community/environmental factors
- Simple, intuitive labeling
- Recognizes that recovery is holistic (Guiding Principle 4)

**Limitations:**
- "Home" as a dimension is somewhat narrow for a mobile app context -- stable housing is critical for recovery but is not readily trackable via self-assessment in the same way as health or community
- "Purpose" conflates vocational, educational, and existential meaning
- No explicit spiritual dimension (spirituality is acknowledged in the guiding principles but not as a standalone dimension)
- No built-in scoring methodology
- Designed as a policy framework, not a self-assessment tool

### 1.4 Biopsychosocial-Spiritual Model

**Source:** George L. Engel, "The Need for a New Medical Model: A Challenge for Biomedicine" (1977). Spiritual extension widely attributed to multiple authors including Christina Puchalski and Harold Koenig.

[Source Type: Journal Article | George L. Engel | 1977 | Science, 196(4286), 129-136 | High]
[Source Type: Encyclopedia | Wikipedia - Biopsychosocial model | current | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biopsychosocial_model | Medium-High]

**Overview:** Engel's 1977 biopsychosocial model challenged the purely biomedical approach to illness, arguing that health and disease are the result of interactions between biological, psychological, and social factors. The model was later extended to include a spiritual dimension, creating the biopsychosocial-spiritual (BPS-S) framework widely used in addiction medicine, palliative care, and holistic health:

| Dimension | Focus | Recovery Mapping |
|---|---|---|
| **Biological** | Physical health, brain chemistry, genetics, medication, nutrition, sleep, exercise | Sobriety, physical withdrawal management, health behaviors, exercise, nutrition, sleep quality |
| **Psychological** | Mental health, emotions, cognition, trauma, coping, self-awareness | Emotional regulation, cognitive distortions, trauma processing, self-esteem, identity work |
| **Social** | Relationships, community, family, peer support, social roles, belonging | Accountability relationships, fellowship, family healing, social reintegration, service |
| **Spiritual** | Meaning, purpose, values, connection to transcendent, hope, faith | Prayer, scripture, church community, Step 3/11 work, surrender, relationship with God |

**Application to addiction:** The biopsychosocial-spiritual model is widely cited in addiction medicine literature. SAMHSA itself acknowledged in its 2012 definition that "recovery is holistic" and that it involves multiple dimensions including spiritual. The American Society of Addiction Medicine (ASAM) criteria for patient placement use a multidimensional assessment that encompasses biological, psychological, social, and (in practice) spiritual dimensions. [FACT | Medium Confidence]

**Strengths:**
- Strong clinical foundation -- rooted in Engel's widely-cited medical model
- Explicitly includes the spiritual dimension, which is central to 12-step recovery
- Directly applicable to addiction medicine assessment
- Recognized by ASAM and major treatment organizations
- Maps cleanly to 4 quadrants for visual self-assessment

**Limitations:**
- Originally a clinical assessment model, not a self-help tool
- No standardized self-assessment scoring methodology
- "Biological" may feel clinical/medical to users
- Does not explicitly address community/systemic factors (absorbed into "Social")

## 3. Clinical Evidence

### 3.1 Holistic/Quadrant Approaches vs. Single-Dimension Programs

The clinical consensus supports multidimensional approaches to addiction recovery:

- **SAMHSA's holistic recovery principle (2012):** "Recovery encompasses an individual's whole life, including mind, body, spirit, and community." This explicitly rejects single-dimension models. [FACT | High Confidence]

- **ASAM multidimensional assessment:** The American Society of Addiction Medicine's patient placement criteria assess six dimensions: (1) acute intoxication/withdrawal, (2) biomedical conditions, (3) emotional/behavioral/cognitive, (4) readiness to change, (5) relapse/continued use, (6) recovery/living environment. This multidimensional approach is the clinical standard of care. [FACT | High Confidence]

- **Project MATCH (1997):** The largest clinical trial of alcohol treatment found that patient-treatment matching across multiple dimensions (cognitive-behavioral, motivational enhancement, 12-step facilitation) produced equivalent outcomes, suggesting that holistic engagement matters more than any single treatment dimension. [FACT | High Confidence]

[Source Type: Government Research | Project MATCH Research Group | 1997 | Journal of Studies on Alcohol, 58(1), 7-29 | High]

- **Relapse prevention models (Marlatt & Gordon, 1985; Marlatt & Donovan, 2005):** These foundational models emphasize that relapse risk increases when multiple life dimensions are simultaneously stressed. The "apparently irrelevant decisions" (AIDs) concept shows how erosion in seemingly unrelated life areas (sleep, exercise, social isolation) creates conditions for relapse. [FACT | High Confidence]

[Source Type: Published Book | Marlatt & Donovan | 2005 | ISBN 978-1593851767 | High]

### 3.2 Balanced Life Approaches in Relapse Prevention

The concept of "lifestyle balance" is central to relapse prevention theory:

- **Marlatt's lifestyle balance model:** G. Alan Marlatt identified lifestyle imbalance (excessive "shoulds" relative to "wants") as a major relapse risk factor. His model recommends monitoring and maintaining balance across physical health, social connection, work/purpose, and leisure/self-care. [FACT | High Confidence]

- **Gorski's CENAPS model:** Terence Gorski's relapse prevention model identifies 37 warning signs organized across physical, emotional, cognitive, social, and behavioral dimensions -- explicitly multidimensional. [FACT | Medium Confidence]

- **Recovery Capital concept (Cloud & Granfield, 2008):** Recovery capital -- the sum total of resources available to support recovery -- is explicitly organized across multiple dimensions: physical (health, housing), human (education, skills, mental health), social (relationships, community), and cultural (values, beliefs). This maps directly to quadrant models. [FACT | High Confidence]

[Source Type: Published Book | Cloud & Granfield | 2008 | ISBN 978-0415942126 | High]

### 3.3 Spiritual Dimension in Recovery Outcomes

Evidence for the role of spirituality/religion in recovery is substantial:

- **12-step program outcomes:** Multiple meta-analyses confirm that active participation in 12-step programs (which centrally involve spiritual practices) is associated with improved sobriety outcomes. The landmark Cochrane review by Kelly et al. (2020) found that Alcoholics Anonymous participation was as effective or more effective than other established treatments, and that spiritual mechanisms of change partially explained outcomes. [FACT | High Confidence]

[Source Type: Systematic Review | Kelly et al. | 2020 | Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews | High]

- **Spiritual awakening as mechanism:** Kelly et al. identified "spiritual/religious practices" as one of several mechanisms through which AA produces its effects, alongside social network changes, self-efficacy, and coping skills. [FACT | High Confidence]

- **Religious coping and recovery:** Koenig (2012) reviewed 3,000+ empirical studies and found that religious involvement is associated with better mental health outcomes, including reduced substance abuse, in the majority of studies. [FACT | Medium Confidence]

[Source Type: Published Book | Harold G. Koenig | 2012 | Handbook of Religion and Health, 2nd ed. | High]

- **Christian-specific recovery evidence:** Celebrate Recovery, the largest Christ-centered recovery program, reports that participants experience significant improvements across all life dimensions. While controlled outcome studies are limited, program-reported data suggests that the holistic spiritual framework enhances engagement and retention. [PRELIMINARY | Low Confidence -- program-reported data, not independent research]

### 3.4 Imbalance Across Dimensions and Relapse Risk

The relationship between dimensional imbalance and relapse risk is well-supported:

- **Carnes' PCI framework:** As documented in the existing PCI research for this project, Patrick Carnes' Personal Craziness Index is built on the explicit premise that lifestyle erosion across multiple dimensions precedes relapse. The PCI tracks 12 dimensions and is designed to detect imbalance before it cascades into the FASTER Scale relapse progression. [FACT | High Confidence -- confirmed in PCI research document]

- **Marlatt's "seemingly irrelevant decisions":** Relapse often begins not with an explicit decision to act out but with a series of small erosions in daily routine and self-care across multiple life areas. Skipping exercise leads to poor sleep, which leads to irritability, which strains relationships, which increases isolation, which creates opportunity for acting out. [FACT | High Confidence]

- **HALT acronym:** The widely-used recovery acronym HALT (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired) maps to four dimensions: physical (hungry/tired), emotional (angry), and social (lonely). When multiple HALT states co-occur, relapse risk multiplies. [FACT | High Confidence]

- **Allostatic load model:** Chronic stress across multiple domains depletes the body's capacity to maintain homeostasis, reducing the neurological resources available for impulse control and recovery-consistent decision-making. This provides a biological mechanism for why multidimensional imbalance increases relapse risk. [INFERENCE | Medium Confidence]

## 4. Digital Implementation

### 4.1 Quadrant Self-Assessment in Mobile Apps

**Scoring approaches:**

- **Likert scale per quadrant (1-10):** The most common approach. Users rate each of the 4 dimensions on a 1-10 scale. Simple, fast (under 30 seconds for 4 ratings), produces a clear profile. [FACT | High Confidence -- standard UX pattern]

- **Behavioral indicator checklists:** Similar to the PCI approach -- provide 3-5 specific behaviors per quadrant and have users check which apply. More objective but slower. [INFERENCE | Medium Confidence]

- **Slider-based assessment:** Visual sliders for each dimension, potentially with anchor descriptions at each end. Provides continuous data but may feel imprecise. [INFERENCE | Medium Confidence]

- **Combined approach (recommended):** A brief (1-2 minute) assessment using both a Likert rating AND 2-3 behavioral indicators per quadrant. The Likert provides the subjective self-assessment; the behavioral indicators ground it in observable reality. This mirrors the relationship between a mood rating (subjective) and a behavioral checklist (objective). [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

**Assessment frequency:**
- **Daily:** Too frequent for a 4-quadrant assessment; creates fatigue alongside the daily LBI check-in
- **Weekly:** Optimal -- provides enough data points for trend analysis without causing assessment fatigue. Aligns with weekly LBI scoring cycle.
- **Bi-weekly or monthly:** Too infrequent for early recovery when conditions change rapidly

### 4.2 Visualization

**Radar/spider chart:** The most natural visualization for 4-dimensional data. Four axes radiating from a center point, with the user's score on each axis connected to form a polygon. A balanced person produces a roughly symmetric polygon; an imbalanced person produces a lopsided one. [FACT | High Confidence -- established data visualization pattern]

**Benefits of radar chart for recovery:**
- Instantly communicates balance vs. imbalance visually
- Shows which quadrant is weakest at a glance
- Historical overlays (this week vs. last week) show directional change
- The "target" polygon (balanced, high scores) provides an aspirational visual
- Works well on mobile screens

**Alternative/complementary visualizations:**
- **Balance wheel:** A divided circle (pie chart variant) where each quadrant fills proportionally to its score. Common in life coaching contexts.
- **Quadrant grid:** A 2x2 grid with each cell colored by score (green/amber/red). Simple but less informative than radar.
- **Trend lines:** Four separate line charts (one per quadrant) showing weekly scores over time. Good for trend analysis but loses the "balance" visual.
- **Stacked area chart:** Shows the relative contribution of each quadrant over time. Good for seeing shifts in balance patterns.

**Recommended approach:** Primary visualization is a radar chart for the current-week snapshot; secondary visualization is a multi-line trend chart for historical analysis. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

### 4.3 Making Tracking Actionable

The critical differentiator between measurement and impact is actionability. Assessment without action is just data collection. The quadrant feature must produce recommendations:

**Imbalance detection:**
- If one quadrant is 3+ points below the average of the other three, flag it as a "needs attention" area
- If all four quadrants are declining week-over-week, trigger a general wellness alert
- If the lowest quadrant drops below a threshold (e.g., 3/10), trigger a specific intervention recommendation

**Quadrant-specific recommendations:**

| Quadrant | If Low | Recommended Actions |
|---|---|---|
| Body/Physical | Score < 5 | Suggest exercise activity, sleep tracking, nutrition check-in, medical appointment |
| Mind/Mental | Score < 5 | Suggest journaling, devotional reading, structured step work, therapy check-in |
| Heart/Relational | Score < 5 | Suggest calling sponsor, accountability check-in, FANOS with spouse, attend meeting |
| Spirit/Spiritual | Score < 5 | Suggest prayer activity, scripture reading, gratitude list, church attendance |

**Integration with existing features:**
- Low Body score -> suggest Exercise activity
- Low Mind score -> suggest Journaling, CBT Thoughts activity
- Low Heart score -> suggest Phone Calls, FANOS check-in
- Low Spirit score -> suggest Prayer, Affirmations, Devotional

This creates a feedback loop where the quadrant assessment drives engagement with other app features. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

### 4.4 Integration with Daily Activities

The Recovery Quadrant should not exist in isolation. Key integration points:

- **Daily Recovery Score:** Quadrant balance score (a composite of the 4 quadrant ratings) should contribute to the overall Daily Recovery Score as a weighted component
- **LBI/PCI correlation:** Quadrant trends should be visible alongside LBI weekly scores, showing the relationship between high-altitude balance (4 quadrants) and ground-level behavioral tracking (7 critical items)
- **FASTER Scale correlation:** A declining quadrant profile should appear alongside FASTER Scale stage history
- **Activity completion:** Completing activities in the app (exercise, prayer, journaling, phone calls) could auto-suggest quadrant improvements
- **Journaling prompts:** Weekly quadrant assessment could generate journal prompts focused on the weakest quadrant

## 6. Relationship to Existing App Features

### 6.1 Recovery Quadrant vs. Life Balance Index (LBI/PCI)

The Recovery Quadrant and LBI operate at different levels of abstraction:

| Aspect | Recovery Quadrant | LBI (PCI) |
|---|---|---|
| **Granularity** | 4 broad life domains | 10 specific behavioral dimensions |
| **Assessment type** | Subjective self-rating + behavioral indicators | Binary behavioral occurrence tracking |
| **Frequency** | Weekly | Daily |
| **Time to complete** | 2-3 minutes | Under 60 seconds |
| **Scoring** | Likert (1-10 per quadrant) | Binary (0/1 per item, 0-49 weekly) |
| **Visualization** | Radar chart + trend lines | Line chart with risk bands |
| **Purpose** | Strategic life balance awareness | Tactical daily behavior monitoring |
| **Actionability** | Recommends categories of action | Flags specific behavioral erosion |
| **Personalization** | Standard quadrants, personalized sub-indicators | Fully user-defined indicators |

**Complementary relationship:** The Quadrant provides a "satellite view" of recovery wellness, while the LBI provides a "street-level view." A user might show a low Quadrant score in the Body dimension while the LBI's Physical Health indicators confirm specific behaviors driving that decline. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

### 6.2 Integration with FASTER Scale

The FASTER Scale measures active relapse progression. The Quadrant measures underlying conditions that create vulnerability to FASTER escalation:

- Declining Quadrant scores (especially across multiple dimensions) should predict subsequent FASTER stage progression
- A user whose Body and Spirit scores are falling may be entering the F (Forgetting priorities) stage of FASTER
- Correlation display: Quadrant trends alongside FASTER stage history on a shared timeline

### 6.3 Integration with Three Circles

Three Circles define behavioral boundaries (inner/middle/outer circle). The Quadrant provides context for why a user might be drifting toward middle-circle behaviors -- an imbalanced life (low Quadrant scores) creates the conditions where middle-circle behaviors become more tempting.

### 6.4 Integration with Daily Recovery Score

The Daily Recovery Score is the app's composite wellness metric. Quadrant data should contribute to this score, weighted appropriately:
- Quadrant balance (how symmetric the four scores are) could be a component
- Individual quadrant scores that fall below thresholds could trigger alerts

## 7. Recommended Approach

### 7.1 Framework Selection: Hybrid Model

**Recommendation:** Use a hybrid framework that combines the best elements of each model:

| Element | Source | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Quadrant labels** | Covey + Mark 12:30 | "Body, Mind, Heart, Spirit" -- intuitive, memorable, scripturally grounded |
| **Clinical grounding** | SAMHSA + BPS-S | Frame the quadrants using SAMHSA's recovery-specific language and BPS-S clinical foundation |
| **Spiritual emphasis** | BPS-S + Mark 12:30 | Explicit spiritual dimension with Christian content, which SAMHSA lacks |
| **Self-assessment method** | Original (informed by all models) | Likert scale + behavioral indicators per quadrant |
| **Scoring** | Original | 1-10 per quadrant, balance score derived from variance |

### 7.2 Quadrant Definitions

| Quadrant | Label | Scripture | SAMHSA Equivalent | BPS-S Equivalent | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Body** | Physical Stewardship | 1 Cor 6:19-20 "temples of the Holy Spirit" | Health | Biological | Physical health, exercise, sleep, nutrition, medical care, energy level |
| **Mind** | Mental Renewal | Romans 12:2 "renewing of your mind" | Purpose (partial) | Psychological | Mental health, learning, emotional regulation, cognitive clarity, therapy engagement |
| **Heart** | Relational Connection | Gal 6:2 "carry each other's burdens" | Community | Social | Relationships, accountability, fellowship, family bonds, isolation vs. connection |
| **Spirit** | Spiritual Vitality | Psalm 42:1 "my soul pants for you" | (implicit in principles) | Spiritual | Prayer life, scripture engagement, church attendance, sense of God's presence, surrender |

### 7.3 Why This Hybrid Works

1. **Body/Mind/Heart/Spirit** is the most intuitive and memorable labeling scheme
2. It maps directly to Mark 12:30 (strength/mind/heart/soul), providing deep scriptural resonance for the Christian user
3. SAMHSA's clinical authority gives the approach credibility beyond just a personal development framework
4. The explicit spiritual dimension (missing from SAMHSA's formal four dimensions) is essential for SA/Celebrate Recovery
5. The four categories are distinct enough to rate separately but comprehensive enough to cover whole-person wellness
6. The framework complements (rather than competes with) the existing LBI feature -- different level of abstraction, different frequency, different purpose

### 7.4 Assessment Design

**Weekly assessment (2-3 minutes):**
1. Rate each quadrant 1-10 with anchor descriptions
2. Select 0-3 behavioral indicators per quadrant that applied this week
3. Optional: one-sentence reflection per quadrant
4. View radar chart + trend line
5. Receive recommendations for weakest quadrant

**Scoring:**
- Individual quadrant scores: 1-10
- Composite balance score: derived from mean and variance of the four scores
- A high mean with low variance = balanced and well (optimal)
- A high mean with high variance = overall well but imbalanced (watch area)
- A low mean = struggling across dimensions (alert)

### 7.5 Differentiation from LBI

To justify both features existing:
- **LBI = behavioral tracking (what you did today):** "Did these warning behaviors occur?"
- **Quadrant = self-assessment (how you feel about each life area this week):** "How would you rate your physical health this week?"
- The two together give a complete picture: LBI catches the behaviors, Quadrant captures the subjective experience
- They should be correlated -- a user whose LBI Physical Health indicators are consistently triggering should also report a low Body quadrant score
- When they diverge (high LBI scores in a domain but high Quadrant self-rating), that may indicate denial or blind spots

## 8. Research Gaps

1. **No controlled studies comparing quadrant-based recovery self-assessment to single-dimension monitoring.** The theoretical support is strong, but empirical evidence for self-assessment frequency and format is limited. [HYPOTHESIS -- needs validation]

2. **Optimal scoring methodology not established.** The 1-10 Likert scale is standard practice but has not been validated specifically for recovery self-assessment. The relationship between self-reported Likert scores and actual recovery outcomes is unknown. [HYPOTHESIS -- needs validation]

3. **Frequency optimization unknown.** Weekly is a reasonable clinical recommendation, but the optimal assessment frequency (daily, weekly, bi-weekly) has not been tested for this specific use case. [HYPOTHESIS -- needs validation]

4. **Balance score formula.** The relationship between quadrant variance (imbalance) and relapse risk has not been empirically quantified. The theoretical basis is sound (Marlatt, Carnes), but the specific formula for converting 4 Likert scores into a single "balance" metric needs user testing. [HYPOTHESIS -- needs validation]

5. **Christian-specific quadrant models.** I could not find any published Christian recovery program that formally uses a four-quadrant self-assessment tool with Body/Mind/Heart/Spirit labeling. Celebrate Recovery and SA address all four dimensions in content but do not formalize them as a tracking/assessment framework. [PRELIMINARY -- single-source gap]
