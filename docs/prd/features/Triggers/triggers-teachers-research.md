# Triggers as teachers, not enemies: a recovery research synthesis

**Unmanaged triggers drive the majority of relapses in the first 90 days, but contemporary clinical science reframes them as decodable signals rather than threats to eliminate — a shift that has direct implications for how recovery software should be built.** In Marlatt's original 1985 taxonomy, 37% of relapses were precipitated by negative emotional states alone, and Hunt's classic curve showed roughly two-thirds of alcohol, heroin, and tobacco patients relapsed within 90 days. Neuroscience explains why: conditioned cues hijack mesolimbic dopamine and, per Grimm and Shaham's incubation-of-craving work, cue-induced craving actually **intensifies** over the first 60 days of abstinence rather than fading. The treatments with the strongest evidence — CBT-based relapse prevention, Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP), 12-step facilitation, and contingency management — all depend on a patient's capacity to **notice, label, and respond to triggers skillfully**. In software, the A-CHESS JAMA Psychiatry 2014 trial established that an app built around trigger-aware GPS geofencing, ecological momentary assessment, and a panic button can cut risky drinking days roughly in half. Everything else in this report flows from these two facts: triggers are the organizing unit of relapse risk, and trigger literacy — not trigger avoidance — is the therapeutic goal that modern recovery apps should be engineered to develop.

## Part 1: The clinical and behavioral science of triggers

### What a trigger actually is

A trigger is any internal or external stimulus that activates craving or drug-seeking via learned Pavlovian associations. The clinical literature organizes triggers along two axes — **internal vs. external**, and by functional category (emotional, physical, environmental, relational, situational, cognitive/spiritual). Internal triggers include the recovery-community **HALT** check (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired), negative affect (stress, shame, boredom), positive or celebratory affect, interoceptive states (pain, fatigue, withdrawal), intrusive urges, and **euphoric recall** — romanticized memories of past use driven by state-dependent memory bias. External triggers include people (using peers, dealers), places (bars, bathrooms, specific neighborhoods), objects (paraphernalia, bottles, lighters, songs), times (Friday nights, paydays, holidays), sensory cues (smells, sounds), and social contexts. Drummond's 2000 typology further distinguishes **exteroceptive, interoceptive, temporal, and compound** cues; Carter and Tiffany's 1999 meta-analysis established that subjective craving reactivity is robust across substances, while physiological reactivity is more variable.

Marlatt's 1985 relapse taxonomy (N=137) remains the clinical reference point: **59% of relapses were intrapersonal** (negative emotions 37%, urges 8%, positive emotions 6%, testing control 4%, negative physical states 4%) and **41% were interpersonal** (social pressure 24%, interpersonal conflict 15%, positive interpersonal 3%). Negative affect is therefore the single largest relapse precipitant across substances — a finding that should anchor any trigger ontology in a clinical app.

### How triggers hijack the brain

Repeated use causes phasic dopamine signaling in the VTA → nucleus accumbens pathway to **shift from the drug itself to predictive cues** (Schultz; Volkow et al., 2016). Childress's landmark 1999 PET study showed cocaine cues selectively activated amygdala and anterior cingulate in users but not controls, confirming that limbic machinery — not conscious choice — underlies cue-elicited craving. Everitt and Robbins' ventral-to-dorsal striatal shift explains the progression to compulsive habit, while Volkow's iRISA model documents the simultaneous prefrontal hypoactivity that weakens top-down inhibition. Kalivas's glutamate homeostasis hypothesis identifies accumbens GLT-1 downregulation as the molecular substrate of reinstated drug-seeking.

**Robinson and Berridge's incentive-sensitization theory** is the most clinically consequential framework: repeated drug exposure produces long-lasting hypersensitization of mesolimbic dopamine, dissociating pathological **"wanting"** (driven by cues) from hedonic **"liking"**. This explains the paradox patients describe constantly — craving drugs they no longer enjoy, even years into abstinence and in the absence of withdrawal. It also explains why avoidance alone fails: the sensitized system responds to cues the user cannot fully predict or eliminate.

**The incubation of craving** (Grimm, Hope, Wise & Shaham, *Nature* 2001) is particularly important for app design. In rats, cue-induced cocaine seeking progressively intensifies over 60 days of forced abstinence, peaks around 2–3 months, and declines after 6. Bedi and Shaham (2011, *Biol Psychiatry*) replicated the phenomenon for cigarette craving in humans, and Parvaz et al. (2016, *JAMA Psychiatry*) showed an inverted-U trajectory across one year of abstinence in 76 cocaine users. **This means apps should assume users' trigger reactivity grows for the first three months of sobriety — the opposite of most users' intuition and the opposite of most app design, which treats day 90 as "safe."**

### The quantitative impact on relapse

Hunt, Barnett and Branch's 1971 relapse curve is the historical anchor: roughly **two-thirds relapsed by 90 days** and ~75% by 12 months across alcohol, heroin, and tobacco. NIDA's contemporary consensus places overall SUD relapse at **40–60%**, comparable to hypertension (50–70% non-adherence) and diabetes (30–50%) — the empirical foundation of the chronic-disease model. **Without maintenance pharmacotherapy, nicotine, opioid, and alcohol one-year relapse rates reach 80–95%.** Risk drops below 15% only after five continuous years of sobriety. Marlatt's classic model was reformulated by Witkiewitz and Marlatt (2004) as a dynamic nonlinear system; Hufford's catastrophe-theory variant predicted **58% (inpatient) and 83% (outpatient) variance** in six-month drinking vs. 19%/14% for linear models — confirming that relapse is a phase-transition phenomenon driven by interacting tonic (vulnerability) and phasic (trigger) inputs, not a linear accumulation of risk.

### How clinicians actually identify triggers

The workhorse is **functional analysis** — the antecedent-behavior-consequence (ABC) chain borrowed from operant conditioning. For each use episode, the clinician documents the "5 Ws" (when, where, why, with whom, what happened), the specific behavior, and both immediate reinforcers (relief, euphoria) and delayed consequences (guilt, financial damage). Validated instruments include the **Inventory of Drug-Taking Situations** (IDTS; Annis & Martin 1985, 50 items mapping Marlatt's categories), the **Situational Confidence Questionnaire**, and substance-specific craving scales — the Penn Alcohol Craving Scale (Cronbach's α 0.92–0.95), the Obsessive-Compulsive Drinking Scale, and the Cocaine Craving Questionnaire. Self-monitoring diaries produce therapeutic **reactivity** — the act of recording changes behavior — and "play the tape forward" recruits prospective episodic memory to counter euphoric recall.

### Reframing triggers as teachers: where the growth-oriented frameworks converge

Third-wave therapies and trauma-informed models share a single move: **the trigger is data, not danger**. ACT teaches cognitive defusion ("I'm having the thought that I need a drink" rather than "I need a drink"), urge acceptance, and values-clarified committed action. MBRP's urge-surfing practice asks the user to approach the craving with curiosity — *what does this wave have to show me?* — rather than avoidance. Internal Family Systems (Schwartz) treats a craving as a **Firefighter part** deploying to extinguish exile pain; the trigger reveals which exile is activated. Gabor Maté reframes the core clinical question from "why the addiction?" to **"why the pain?"**, positioning triggers as portals to early attachment wounds. Post-traumatic growth theory (Tedeschi & Calhoun) documents that 50–67% of adversity survivors report growth across five PTGI domains; Krentzman (2013) showed that AA-affiliated individuals with ≥12 months sobriety outperform the general population on gratitude, spirituality, and optimism.

The evidence base for this reframe is strongest inside MBRP. **Bowen et al. (2014, *JAMA Psychiatry*, N=286)** randomized post-intensive-treatment patients to MBRP, cognitive-behavioral RP, or 12-step TAU. Both MBRP and RP cut relapse risk by ~54% and heavy-drinking risk by ~59% vs. TAU at six months; critically, **at 12 months MBRP outperformed both RP and TAU** on substance-use days and heavy-drinking days, suggesting that the "trigger as teacher" stance produces more durable skill generalization than coping-skills training alone.

### Which framework actually works best

No single framework is dominant. The evidence supports a **stacked, integrated approach**, matched to patient characteristics:

| Framework | Core trigger technique | Strongest evidence | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CBT / Relapse Prevention** | Functional analysis, restructuring, coping-skills training, AVE management | Magill & Ray 2009 meta-analysis g=0.154 overall, 0.79 vs. no treatment; Magill et al. 2019 replication | Structural core of any treatment |
| **TSF / 12-step** | HALT, "people/places/things," sponsor calls, meetings as pro-recovery cue network | Kelly, Humphreys & Ferri 2020 Cochrane — **superior to CBT** for continuous abstinence; cost-saving | Clients with pro-drinking social networks |
| **MBRP** | Urge surfing, SOBER breathing, decentering | Bowen 2014 JAMA Psychiatry — **MBRP beats standard RP at 12 months** | Durable long-term relapse prevention |
| **DBT-SUD** | TIPP, distress tolerance, dialectical abstinence | Harned 2008 — 87.5% vs. 33.3% remission | Borderline PD + SUD comorbidity |
| **Contingency Management** | Voucher/prize reinforcement of verified abstinence | Prendergast 2006 d=0.42; Ginley 2021 durable OR=1.22 at 1 year | Stimulants and opioids |
| **ACT** | Defusion, urge willingness, values-based action | Ruiz-Jiménez 2024 — superior to CBT at end-of-treatment; equivalent long-term | High experiential avoidance, values disconnect |
| **MI / MET** | Change talk, importance/confidence rulers | Hettema 2005 meta-analysis; best as engagement adjunct | Entry into care, ambivalence |

**The consensus recommendation**: engage with MI, deliver **CBT-based trigger skills** as the structural core, layer **MBRP or ACT** for durable 12-month prevention and the trigger-as-teacher reframe, facilitate **ongoing TSF/AA or SMART** mutual-help for free lifetime social support, add **contingency management** for stimulants/opioids, combine with pharmacotherapy (naltrexone, acamprosate, buprenorphine, methadone), and deploy **DBT or trauma-focused modalities (IFS, Maté's compassionate inquiry)** for dysregulated or trauma-laden presentations. This matches NIDA/SAMHSA/NIAAA's "no wrong door," stepped, personalized chronic-care model.

## Part 2: Triggers in recovery software

### The current app landscape

The recovery-app market spans a wide quality gradient: FDA-cleared prescription digital therapeutics (reSET/reSET-O), evidence-based platforms with published RCTs (A-CHESS/CHESS Health Connections, DynamiCare, Pelago), virtual clinics (Workit, Ria Health), harm-reduction tools (Try Dry, Daybreak, Hello Sunday Morning), and massive consumer trackers with essentially no controlled evidence (I Am Sober, Nomo, SoberTool, the now-defunct Sober Grid). Tofighi et al.'s 2019 JMIR review of 904 recovery apps found **very few integrated evidence-based content** — most rely on timers, motivational quotes, or crowdsourced tips unaligned with any therapeutic framework. **Retention is the field's defining failure**: Baumel et al.'s 2019 JMIR study of 93 Android mental-health apps found median 15-day retention of 3.9% and 30-day retention of 3.3%. Peer-support and mindfulness apps meaningfully outperform trackers.

### How the leading apps actually handle triggers

| App | Trigger logging | Intensity | GPS/context alerts | In-app coping | Framework | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A-CHESS / Connections** | Weekly check-ins + user-defined high-risk location logs | Yes, triggers counselor alerts | Yes — geofenced bars/risk locations | Panic button, relaxation audio | CBT + MI + SDT | Gustafson 2014 JAMA Psych RCT, n=349 |
| **reSET-O** | Logs substance use, cravings, triggers to clinician dashboard | Yes | No GPS | 31+30 CBT lessons, CM rewards | CBT + CRA + CM | Maricich 2021: 77.3% vs 62.1% abstinence |
| **DynamiCare** | Behavior logs + self-tests | Test results | GPS confirms appointments; random test prompts | 90+ CBT modules; CM Guide coach | Contingency Management | Feasibility RCT: 24% vs 3% 90-day retention |
| **Pelago (Quit Genius)** | Check-ins + craving log | Yes | Scheduled coach | CBT, MAT, remote biosensors | CBT + MAT + CM | 15 peer-reviewed pubs |

**Two facts dominate this market.** First, the apps with the deepest trigger curricula have the strongest evidence but historically the smallest user bases, while the most-downloaded apps (I Am Sober, Nomo, Sober Grid) have effectively no controlled trials. Second, the prescription-digital-therapeutic business model broke in 2023: **Pear Therapeutics filed Chapter 11 in April 2023**, its assets sold at auction for **$6.05M** against its 2021 SPAC valuation of $1.6B, after payers refused to reimburse its validated software. PursueCare acquired reSET/reSET-O and relaunched in August 2024 at **$90 out-of-pocket**, a complete inversion of the insurer-prescribed model. Sober Grid went defunct in 2023–2025 following founder Beau Mann's death. The market is simultaneously mature in features and unstable in distribution.

### The gold standard: why A-CHESS still defines the field

Gustafson et al.'s 2014 JAMA Psychiatry RCT (N=349 post-residential AUD patients, 8-month intervention plus 4-month follow-up) found the A-CHESS group had **1.39 vs. 2.75 risky drinking days per month** (mean difference 1.37; 95% CI 0.46–2.27; p=0.003), with a dose-response relationship between app engagement and outcome. The architecture is the field's most mature trigger system: **user-defined geofenced high-risk locations** automatically push relaxation audio and a peer-outreach dialog; **weekly BAM-style check-ins** trigger counselor alerts when scores worsen; a **panic button** notifies the nearest peers via GPS with bridge interventions. A-CHESS has been extended to OUD (Gustafson 2023 *Am J Psychiatry*), alcoholic liver disease, and partners (Partner-CHESS). **It is the empirical template any serious trigger-management feature should study.**

### The JITAI framework as the design backbone

The dominant scientific framework for building real-time trigger-responsive interventions is the **Just-in-Time Adaptive Intervention (JITAI)**, articulated by Nahum-Shani et al. (2018, *Annals of Behavioral Medicine*). A JITAI specifies six components that should map to every trigger feature you build: **distal outcome** (sustained abstinence), **proximal outcome** (reduced craving in the next hour, completion of a coping exercise), **decision points** (when the system evaluates whether to act), **tailoring variables** (craving intensity, location, affect, social context, time since last EMA, sleep), **intervention options** (psychoeducation, urge-surfing audio, breathing, peer chat, sponsor call, panic button), and **decision rules** (if-then logic or learned policies). Carpenter, Menictas, Nahum-Shani, Wetter and Murphy (2020) review five addiction JITAIs and three micro-randomized trials and provide direct addiction guidance.

**Empirically validated tailoring variables** — which should drive your data model — are consistent across substances. Businelle's Smart-T work, Hébert et al. (2021), and Wyant et al. (2024) found that **craving, stress, substance availability, self-efficacy/confidence, recent stressful events, proximity to using peers, affect, and permissiveness of location** predicted imminent lapse with AUCs of 0.89–0.93 for week, day, and hour-level alcohol-lapse prediction in small samples. The robust four-variable core — **craving + negative affect + contextual availability + self-efficacy** — should be captured on every EMA.

### A recommended data model for triggers

A clinically sound schema should represent a trigger as a structured event with these fields:

- **Event metadata**: timestamp, app-session context, EMA-prompted vs. user-initiated
- **Category**: internal vs. external, with sub-tags across emotional, physical, environmental, relational, situational, cognitive/spiritual (mirroring Marlatt's IDTS categories so therapists can interpret patterns)
- **Intensity**: 0–10 VAS for craving, plus PACS-style short scales weekly; always allow "not now" responses without penalty
- **Context**: location class (user-tagged high-risk vs. neutral vs. safe), people present, activity, preceding event, interoceptive state (HALT check)
- **Affect**: valence + arousal or brief PANAS items
- **Outcome**: used / near-miss / coped successfully / which coping skill / duration of urge
- **Linkages**: ability to link triggers into **chains** (preceding events, seemingly irrelevant decisions) and **co-occurrence clusters** across time

Intensity should use validated instruments where practical — the **Penn Alcohol Craving Scale** (5 items, α 0.92–0.95) weekly plus single-item VAS during EMA — rather than idiosyncratic scales.

### UX patterns that work

Quick-log and deep-log should coexist. A **one-tap urge button** with intensity slider must be reachable within one screen, because craving peaks are brief (typically 20–30 minutes) and cognitive load is high. Structured logging should happen during EMA prompts or post-episode reviews. **Mood + trigger co-logging** — as in I Am Sober — reduces cognitive load and captures the negative-affect driver that Marlatt identified as the largest relapse category. **Conversational interfaces** reduce friction for some users but lose structured data; a hybrid template that uses natural language with structured extraction is the best current approach.

Visualization should make patterns legible: **heatmaps of trigger frequency by time-of-day and day-of-week**, **correlation insights** (craving × HALT state × location), **trigger chain diagrams**, and weekly review rituals that surface top-3 patterns rather than overwhelm. **Avoid harsh sobriety-counter resets** on relapse — the Abstinence Violation Effect literature shows this directly feeds relapse by triggering shame and loss-of-control beliefs. Frame resets as **"learning moments"** or progress points, aligning the UX with the trigger-as-teacher reframe.

### Intervention and notification design

When a trigger is logged, the app should deploy **evidence-based content proportioned to intensity**. At low intensity, a brief mindfulness or cognitive reframe is sufficient; at high intensity, escalate to urge-surfing audio (MBRP's SOBER breathing is rated helpful by >82% of users in MBRP trials), grounding exercises, contingency-management reward reminders, peer chat, sponsor/supporter contact, and finally a **Stanley-Brown digital safety plan** given the elevated suicide comorbidity in SUD (Stanley & Brown 2018 *JAMA Psychiatry* showed 45% fewer suicidal behaviors post-ED with their safety planning intervention).

Notification design is consequential: **Bidargaddi et al. 2018** and the Drink Less micro-randomized trial found that tailored notifications increase 24-hour proximal engagement by ~8.8%, with peak receptivity weekend afternoons/evenings; effects decay over weeks, so **frequency caps, user quiet hours, and event-triggered (not clock-triggered) notifications** are essential to avoid fatigue. **Geofencing works but is imperfect** — Wray et al. 2019 found ~40% of geofence-triggered surveys arrived when users were not at the target location; user-set geofences outperform generic "all bars" fences (62% vs. 49% accuracy). Always prefer user-defined over generic high-risk locations.

### Personalization, ML, and the optimization pipeline

A responsible personalization pipeline should **start with theory-driven decision rules** (e.g., if craving ≥ 6 AND within geofence, then push urge-surfing audio AND offer sponsor contact), instrument for a **micro-randomized trial (MRT)** to estimate time-varying causal effects (Klasnja et al. 2015; Boruvka et al. 2018's EMEE estimator), and evolve toward **contextual bandits or Thompson sampling** for per-user personalization — the architecture used in HeartSteps V2 and increasingly in addiction JITAIs.

Cautions: predictive-lapse models have shown high AUCs in small samples (N=26–151) and feature importance is unstable; van Genugten et al.'s 2025 systematic review found deployed "JITAIs" often lack core adaptivity and receptivity components. Cold-start, label noise, and the small-per-user sample problem are real. In a vulnerable population, **false-positive alerts carry real harm** (alert fatigue, stigma, over-surveillance), which argues for conservative thresholds, human-in-the-loop for consequential interventions, and transparent explanations of why a notification was sent.

### Privacy, trauma-informed design, and the 42 CFR Part 2 rule

**Regulatory baseline**: SUD-specific records are governed by **42 CFR Part 2**, whose final rule was published February 2024 and whose enforcement began **February 16, 2026** — meaning any clinic-integrated app is presently bound. The updated rule aligns with HIPAA on consent, breaches, and penalties; permits a single consent for treatment/payment/operations; adds a new "SUD counseling notes" analogue to HIPAA psychotherapy notes; and changes required redisclosure language. Consumer-direct apps may fall outside Part 2 but face the **FTC Health Breach Notification Rule**, state laws (California CMIA, Washington's My Health My Data Act), GDPR for EU users, and HIPAA when integrated with covered entities.

Practical security minimums: end-to-end encryption for peer and clinician messaging; local-only storage options for sensitive free-text (euphoric-recall content, trauma narratives); on-device geofence matching rather than streaming location to the cloud; pseudonymization for research data; clear user export and deletion paths; and explicit, plain-language disclosures of what is collected and who sees it.

**Trauma-informed content design** (SAMHSA's six principles adapted to digital) should govern every copy and notification decision: avoid re-traumatizing imagery and stigmatizing labels ("addict," "clean/dirty"); include content warnings before user-uploaded material; moderate peer content to discourage **"war stories"** and euphoric recall that can destabilize readers; provide a **safe exit** (quick-close/fake screen) for users in unsafe relationships; prominently display 988 and SAMHSA crisis lines; co-design with people in recovery and maintain a consumer advisory board. **Assessment reactivity is real** — EMA questions themselves can cue craving in smoking-cessation qualitative work — so briefer, well-timed assessments and "not now" options matter.

### Ten best-practice recommendations for a trigger-management feature

1. **Adopt the JITAI framework explicitly** and map every feature to distal/proximal outcomes, decision points, tailoring variables, intervention options, and decision rules.
2. **Structure triggers using Marlatt's IDTS categories** with internal/external × emotional/physical/environmental/relational/situational tagging so therapists can interpret patterns.
3. **Capture the empirically validated tailoring core** — craving, negative affect, contextual availability, and self-efficacy — on every EMA, using PACS-style scales rather than idiosyncratic measures.
4. **Offer both quick-log and deep-log UX** with one-tap urge buttons reachable in one screen and mood+trigger co-logging as the default.
5. **Build a content library from evidence-based sources**: MBRP (urge surfing, SOBER breathing), CBT coping cards, HALT self-check, Stanley-Brown safety plan, peer chat, and a panic button.
6. **Use user-defined geofences, not generic ones**, and expect ~40% false-positive rates; design escalation that fails gracefully.
7. **Personalize conservatively**: theory-driven rules first, MRT instrumentation, contextual bandits later; keep humans in the loop for high-stakes interventions.
8. **Frame the UX around trigger-as-teacher**: avoid shame-inducing counter resets, surface pattern insights as learning moments, and draw language from MBRP and ACT rather than pure avoidance framing.
9. **Expect severe attrition**. Median 30-day mental-health-app retention is 3.3%; build minimum-viable value into session one and week one. Peer community and mindfulness content retain better than trackers.
10. **Design for 42 CFR Part 2, trauma-informed principles, and suicide comorbidity from day one.** Integrate a digital safety plan; minimize location retention; moderate peer content; prominently display crisis resources.

## Conclusion: what changes if you take the science seriously

The most consequential insight across both literatures is that the clinical frameworks with the strongest long-term evidence (MBRP, ACT, and the mindfulness-integrated variants of CBT) and the most successful software implementations (A-CHESS, reSET-O) converge on the same conceptual move: **they stop treating triggers as failures to be prevented and start treating them as data to be decoded**. Grimm and Shaham's incubation findings make clear that triggers will not politely diminish during early abstinence — they intensify — so avoidance as a primary strategy is biologically untenable. Marlatt's finding that negative affect alone drives 37% of relapses means a good trigger system is also a good affect-regulation system. Bowen's 2014 result that MBRP beat standard relapse prevention specifically at 12 months suggests the curiosity-based stance generalizes better than skill drills. And Gustafson's A-CHESS result shows that when software operationalizes these insights — geofenced context awareness, panic-button peer outreach, counselor-alerting check-ins — it can nearly halve risky drinking days.

The gap between what we know and what most apps ship is therefore an opportunity. The consumer market is dominated by timer-and-quote apps with no controlled evidence; the evidence-based end of the market is distributionally fragile (Pear's collapse, Sober Grid's disappearance). A well-designed trigger feature — structured data model aligned with IDTS/Marlatt, JITAI-based intervention logic anchored in MBRP and CBT, user-defined geofencing, trauma-informed copy, and a 42 CFR Part 2-compliant privacy stance — is simultaneously a clinical, commercial, and ethical opportunity. The phrase "trigger as teacher" is not a marketing frame but a translation of three decades of neuroscience and psychotherapy research into product language. Software that takes it literally has both the best chance of working and the best chance of being worth using.
