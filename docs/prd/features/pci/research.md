## Executive Summary

The Personal Craziness Index (PCI) is a self-assessment tool created by Dr. Patrick Carnes that measures overall life manageability across twelve behavioral dimensions. It first appeared in *A Gentle Path through the Twelve Steps* (1989/1993, revised 2012) and is presented in detail with worksheets in *Facing the Shadow* (2005, 3rd edition 2015, pp. 278-287). The PCI is built on two core assumptions: (1) craziness first appears in routine behaviors that support lifestyle balance, and (2) behavioral signs occur in patterns across different parts of life. Users define their own warning indicators across 12 life dimensions, select their 7 most critical, and track them daily with a binary scoring system that produces a weekly total (0-49) mapped to five risk levels from Optimal Health to Very High Risk. The PCI is fundamentally a personalized, upstream relapse-prevention tool -- it detects lifestyle imbalance before it cascades into the emotional/behavioral escalation that tools like the FASTER Scale measure. Multiple sources confirm it is widely used in sex addiction recovery, 12-step programs, and broader therapeutic contexts for resilience monitoring.

## 2. Purpose in Recovery

### The Problem PCI Solves

The PCI addresses a specific gap in recovery awareness: people in recovery (and people in general) often fail to notice that their **life management** is deteriorating until it cascades into a crisis or relapse. They get caught up in big-picture concerns while fundamental daily routines erode. [FACT | High Confidence]

Carnes explains this with a vivid illustration (from *A Gentle Path*, p. 243):

> "Thus, we can be caught up in issues of cosmic importance and not notice that our checking account is overdrawn. If our checking account is overdrawn, we are probably out of socks as well, because we have not done our laundry. If this pattern is pervasive, there is a risk that our lives will become emotionally bankrupt as well -- cosmic issues notwithstanding."

[Source: Integrity Counseling Group blog, directly quoting Carnes | 2021-11-18 | integritycounselinggroup.com | Medium-High]

### Two Core Assumptions

The PCI is built on two foundational assumptions (confirmed verbatim across 5+ independent sources):

1. **"Craziness first appears in routine, simple behaviors that support lifestyle balance."**
2. **"Behavioral signs will occur in patterns involving different parts of our lives."**

[FACT | High Confidence -- verbatim quote confirmed in PA Cert Board PDF, Dawesome Recovery PDF, Recovery Behind Bars PDF, Integrity Counseling Group blog, Augustine Recovery website]

### The "Boulder" Metaphor

Carnes uses the metaphor of a boulder rolling downhill. The PCI's warning signs indicate "the boulder is ready to roll." This captures the accelerating nature of lifestyle deterioration -- once it starts, it gains momentum. Early intervention (when the boulder has barely moved) is far easier than late intervention (when it is already rolling). [FACT | High Confidence]

From the PCI directions: "These are warnings that the boulder is ready to roll. Thus, it will be by your own standards that you will prepare yourself."

[Source: PA Cert Board PDF | pacertboard.org | Medium-High]
[Source: Recovery Behind Bars PDF | recoverybehindbars.com | Medium-High]
[Source: Dawesome Recovery PDF | dawesomerecovery.com | Medium-High]

### Why Carnes Recommends It

The PCI directions state that regular recording and journaling will help users:
- Stay focused on priorities that keep life manageable
- Work on program efforts a day at a time
- Expand knowledge of personal patterns
- Provide a warning of self-destructive cycles during periods of vulnerability

[FACT | High Confidence]

[Source: Recovery Behind Bars PDF | recoverybehindbars.com | Medium-High]
[Source: Dawesome Recovery PDF | dawesomerecovery.com | Medium-High]

## 3. Structure and Categories: The Twelve Dimensions

The PCI consists of **12 life dimensions**, each with space for **3 personal indicators** (some sources say 2-4, but the standard worksheets from *Facing the Shadow* provide 3 blank lines per category). This yields approximately **36 total indicators**. The text explicitly references "the thirty-six or more signs of personal craziness you recorded." [FACT | High Confidence]

### Complete Category List with Descriptions and Example Behaviors

The following is transcribed verbatim from the *Facing the Shadow* (2015) worksheets as reproduced in the Recovery Behind Bars and Dawesome Recovery PDFs, confirmed by the user-provided PA Cert Board images:

---

**1. Physical Health**

Description: "The ultimate insanity is to not take care of our body. When our physical health deteriorates, we have nothing. Yet, we seem to have little time for physical conditioning."

Example behaviors: Being over a certain weight, missing regular exercise for two days or more, smoking cigarettes, feeling exhausted from lack of sleep, skipping showers or meals, not taking medication as prescribed.

Prompt question: *"How do you know that you are not taking care of your body?"*

---

**2. Transportation**

Description: "How people get from place to place is often a statement about lifestyle."

Example behaviors: Car owner who seldom comes to a full stop, routinely exceeds the speed limit, runs out of gas, forgets to check the oil, puts off needed repairs, neglects to clean out the backseat, averages three speeding tickets and ten parking tickets per year. Bus rider who routinely misses the bus, forgets to carry change or bus pass, frequently leaves a smartphone or purse on the bus.

Prompt question: *"What are the transportation behaviors that indicate your life is getting out of control?"*

---

**3. Environment**

Description: "To not have time to do your personal chores is a comment on the order of your life."

Example behaviors: Plants go unwatered, fish are unfed, grocery supplies are depleted, laundry is not done or put away, cleaning is neglected, dishes go unwashed.

Prompt question: *"What are ways in which you neglect your home or living space?"*

---

**4. Work**

Description: "Chaos at work is risky for recovery."

Example behaviors: Failing to return phone calls within twenty-four hours, showing up late for appointments, falling behind in promised work, having an unmanageable in-basket, putting "too many irons in the fire."

Prompt question: *"When your life is unmanageable at work, what are your behaviors?"*

---

**5. Interests**

Description: "What are some positive interests, besides work, that give you perspective on the world? Music, reading, photography, fishing, cooking, gardening are examples."

**IMPORTANT NOTE:** "This is the only category in which you list **positive** factors. If you use one of these for your PCI calculation (later in the exercise) phrase it in that space as 'Lack of ______.'"

Prompt question: *"What are you doing when you're not overextended?"*

---

**6. Social Life**

Description: "Think of friends in your social network (beyond a significant other and family members) who provide significant support for you."

Prompt question: *"What are signs that you've become isolated, alienated, or disconnected from this network?"*

---

**7. Family and Significant Others**

Description: "Examples of craziness in this area of life are staying silent, becoming overtly hostile, or engaging in passive-aggressive behaviors."

Prompt question: *"What behaviors indicate that you are disconnected from those closest to you?"*

---

**8. Finances**

Description: "We handle our financial resources much like our personal resources. Signs of financial overextension include an unbalanced checking account, overdue bills, a lack of ready cash, and spending more than you earn. These may have parallels in various forms of emotional overextension."

Prompt question: *"What signs indicate that you are financially overextended?"*

---

**9. Spiritual Life and Personal Reflection**

Description: "Spirituality can be diverse and include such activities as meditation, yoga, and prayer. Personal reflection includes keeping a personal journal, completing daily readings, and pursuing therapy."

Prompt question: *"What sources of routine personal reflection do you neglect when you are overextended?"*

---

**10. Other Compulsive or Symptomatic Behaviors**

Description: "Compulsive behaviors that have negative consequences indicate something about your general well-being and state of overall recovery. When you watch inordinate amounts of TV, overeat, bite your nails, or develop another compulsive behavior that erodes your self-esteem, these can be signs of burnout or possible unhealthy coping mechanisms. Symptomatic behaviors such as forgetfulness, slips of the tongue, or jealousy are further evidence of overextension."

Prompt question: *"What negative compulsive or symptomatic behaviors are present when you feel 'on the edge'?"*

---

**11. Recovery Practice and Therapeutic Self-Care** (also labeled "Twelve Step Practice and Therapeutic Self-Care")

Description: "Living a Twelve Step way of life involves many practices. Group attendance, Step work, sponsorship, service, and Twelve Step phone calls to support group members become the foundation of a good recovery. For some partners attending therapy, their method of self-care may be being involved in a facilitated support group."

Prompt question: *"What recovery activities do you neglect first?"*

---

**12. Healthy Relationships**

Description: "Engaging in exploitative, abusive, or otherwise unhealthy relationships is a sign that you are not well grounded in your recovery."

Prompt question: *"What are signs that a relationship of yours is unhealthy or becoming unhealthy? Are you lying to the other person in the relationship or leaving out important pieces of the truth? Are you afraid to say what's on your mind? Are your boundaries still firm, or are you doing things someone else wants you to do even when it's not right for you?"*

## 4. How It Works: Mechanics and Scoring

### Phase 1: Setup (One-Time, Periodic Revision)

1. User works through each of the 12 dimensions
2. For each dimension, user writes 2-4 personal behavioral indicators (standard worksheets provide 3 blank lines)
3. This yields approximately 36 total signs of personal craziness
4. User selects the **7 most critical** indicators from across all dimensions ("My critical incidents -- seven key signs of personal craziness")

[FACT | High Confidence]

### Phase 2: Daily Tracking

1. At the end of each day, user reviews their 7 critical items
2. User assigns **one point to each behavior that occurred** during the day
3. Daily score range: 0-7
4. **Critical rule:** "If you fail to record the number of points each day, that day receives an automatic score of seven. If you cannot even do a daily scoring, you are obviously out of balance."

[FACT | High Confidence -- confirmed verbatim in both Recovery Behind Bars and Dawesome Recovery PDFs]

### Phase 3: Weekly Totaling

1. At the end of the week, total the seven daily scores
2. Weekly score range: **0 to 49** (7 items x 7 days)
3. Mark the weekly total on the **PCI Graph** (a 12-week tracking grid)

### Phase 4: 12-Week Charting

The PCI includes two tracking artifacts:

1. **Personal Craziness Chart** -- a 7-row (days of week + total) x 12-column (weeks) grid for recording daily scores
2. **PCI Graph** -- a 5-row (risk levels) x 12-column (weeks) grid for plotting weekly totals, providing a visual trend line

Users chart progress over a twelve-week period and reflect on recovery trajectory.

[FACT | High Confidence -- both charts are reproduced identically in the Recovery Behind Bars and Dawesome Recovery PDFs]

### Interpretation Scale (Weekly Totals)

| Weekly Score | Risk Level | Behavioral Description |
|---|---|---|
| **0-9** | **Optimal Health** | Very resilient. Knows limits; has clear priorities; congruent with values; rooted in diversity; supportive; has established a personal system; balanced, orderly; resolves crises quickly; capacity to sustain spontaneity; shows creative discipline. |
| **10-19** | **Stable Solidity** | Resilient. Recognizes human limits; does not pretend to be more than they are; maintains most boundaries; well ordered; typically feels competent; feels supported; able to weather crisis. |
| **20-29** | **Medium Risk** | Often rushed; can't get it all in; no emotional margin for crisis; vulnerable to slip into old patterns; typically lives as if he or she has inordinate influence over others and/or feels inadequate. |
| **30-39** | **High Risk** | Living in extremes (overactive or inactive); relationships abbreviated; feels irresponsible and is; constantly has reasons for not following through; lives one way, talks another; works hard to catch up. |
| **40-49** | **Very High Risk** | Usually pursuing self-destructive behavior; overzealous about one's special interests; blames others for failures; seldom produces on time; controversial in community; success vs. achievement-oriented. |

[FACT | High Confidence -- interpretation table confirmed verbatim in Recovery Behind Bars PDF, Dawesome Recovery PDF, and user-provided images]

The Recovery Behind Bars version uses 1-10, 11-20, 21-30, 31-40, 41-49. This is a minor difference (off-by-one at boundaries). ## 5. Personalization: How Users Define Their Own Warning Signs

### Design Philosophy

The PCI's personalization is its defining characteristic. From the standard directions (confirmed across all worksheet sources):

> "The process of creating your own PCI is designed to be as value-free as possible. Each person uses the index by setting their own criteria. In other words, discover the behavioral signs that in your experience indicate that you are 'losing it', 'getting out of hand' or 'feeling burnt out.' These are warnings that the boulder is ready to roll. Thus, it will be by your own standards that you will prepare yourself."

[FACT | High Confidence -- verbatim quote confirmed in PA Cert Board, Recovery Behind Bars, and Dawesome Recovery sources]

### Key Personalization Elements

1. **Self-defined indicators:** Users write their own behavioral signs for each dimension. The tool provides categories, descriptions, and example behaviors, but the actual tracked items are entirely user-generated.

2. **Self-selected critical 7:** From approximately 36 indicators, the user chooses the 7 that are most personally significant for detecting their own craziness patterns.

3. **Evolution over time:** "The PCI is especially useful early on as recovery habits are established. Also, the PCI becomes helpful during periods of stress and vulnerability. Many simply use it as a daily reminder of their progress. **These users change the items as they move forward in their recovery.**"

4. **Category 5 reversal:** The Interests category is the only one listing positive behaviors. If a user selects one of these as a critical-7 item, they must rephrase it as "Lack of ______" so it functions as a negative indicator consistent with the scoring system (a point = something went wrong).

[FACT | High Confidence]

### Why Personalization Matters

From Integrity Counseling Group: "The PCI is highly personal. For some people, skipping workouts may not be a big deal. But for you, it might be an indication that you are not taking care of yourself or finding the right balance in life."

[Source: Integrity Counseling Group | 2021-11-18 | integritycounselinggroup.com | Medium-High]

This personalization means the PCI is not a standardized psychometric instrument -- it is a structured self-awareness framework. Its power comes from forcing users to articulate their own early warning signs, not from normative scoring against a population. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

## 7. Relationship to Relapse Prevention

### Position in the Relapse Prevention Cycle

The PCI sits at the **earliest stage** of relapse prevention -- before any relapse-specific behaviors have begun. It monitors the **soil conditions** that allow relapse to take root. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

```
Life in Balance (PCI low) 
    --> Lifestyle erosion begins (PCI rising)
        --> Emotional/behavioral warning signs (FASTER: F, A, S stages)
            --> Escalation (FASTER: T, E stages)
                --> Acting out / Relapse (FASTER: R stage)
```

### PCI as Upstream Prevention

From Transformations Press: "Detecting mild imbalance before we get pulled under is much easier than restoring balance after we've been toppled."

The PCI operates on the principle that **a person whose daily routines are intact is far less likely to begin the relapse progression**. Conversely, a person whose routines are eroding is creating the conditions where forgetting priorities (the F in FASTER) becomes inevitable.

[INFERENCE | High Confidence -- logical derivation from documented PCI purpose and FASTER Scale structure]

### Complementary Tool Relationships

| Tool | What It Monitors | When It Helps |
|---|---|---|
| **PCI** | Daily routine / lifestyle balance | Before any relapse signs appear |
| **3 Circles** | Behavioral boundaries (inner/middle/outer) | When considering specific actions |
| **FASTER Scale** | Emotional/behavioral escalation stage | When noticing emotional distress or behavioral changes |
| **Urge Log** | Specific urge events | When experiencing active urges |
| **Mood Tracking** | Emotional state | Ongoing emotional awareness |

The PCI is the **most proactive** tool in this ecosystem -- it detects deterioration in life management that, if unaddressed, creates the conditions for everything else to escalate. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

## 8. Typical Categories: Detailed Analysis of Behavioral Domains

### Mapping PCI Dimensions to Common Recovery Frameworks

The 12 PCI dimensions can be grouped into broader domains:

**Physical Self-Care:**
- Physical Health (sleep, exercise, diet, hygiene, medication)

**Practical Life Management:**
- Transportation (vehicle care, commute habits)
- Environment (home maintenance, cleanliness)
- Work (professional responsibility, time management)
- Finances (money management, spending)

**Relational Health:**
- Social Life (friendship network, social engagement)
- Family & Significant Others (intimate relationships, family connection)
- Healthy Relationships (boundary maintenance, honesty)

**Internal/Spiritual Health:**
- Interests (hobbies, perspective, enjoyment)
- Spiritual Life & Personal Reflection (prayer, meditation, journaling, therapy)

**Recovery-Specific:**
- Recovery Practice & Therapeutic Self-Care (meetings, step work, sponsorship)
- Other Compulsive or Symptomatic Behaviors (cross-addiction, symptomatic behaviors)

### Notable Observations About the Categories

1. **No explicit "Sexual Behaviors" category:** Unlike what might be expected for a sex addiction recovery tool, the PCI does not include a dedicated sexual behavior dimension. This is intentional -- the PCI monitors **lifestyle conditions** that precede sexual acting out, not the acting out itself. Sexual acting out would be captured by the 3 Circles or the FASTER Scale's Relapse stage. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

2. **No explicit "Emotional/Mood" category:** Mood is not tracked directly. Instead, mood is reflected indirectly through behavioral indicators (compulsive behaviors, relationship withdrawal, neglect of self-care). This is consistent with the PCI's behavioral rather than emotional focus. [INFERENCE | High Confidence]

3. **No explicit "Isolation" category:** Isolation is captured through the Social Life dimension and Family & Significant Others dimension rather than as a standalone category. [FACT | High Confidence]

4. **Transportation as a lifestyle proxy:** This is one of the more unusual categories. Carnes uses transportation habits as a proxy for overall lifestyle chaos -- the idea that how you drive reflects how you live. This may feel dated or less relevant for some users. [INFERENCE | Medium Confidence]

5. **"Interests" as the sole positive category:** This is a deliberate design choice. The PCI primarily tracks what goes wrong, but the Interests category tracks what goes right (engagement in healthy activities). When selected as a critical-7 item, it must be rephrased as an absence ("Lack of reading," "Lack of exercise") to be scoreable. [FACT | High Confidence]

### Categories Mentioned in the User's Original Question That Do Not Exist as Separate PCI Dimensions

- **Emotional/mood indicators** -- Not a separate dimension. Mood is reflected behaviorally.
- **Sexual behaviors** -- Not a separate dimension. Intentionally upstream of sexual acting out.
- **Isolation patterns** -- Not a separate dimension. Captured within Social Life and Family dimensions.

## 10. Digital Implementation Considerations

### Fidelity Requirements: What Must Be True

Based on the research, a digital PCI that is faithful to Carnes' intent must:

1. **Preserve full personalization.** The user MUST define their own indicators. Pre-populated checklists would fundamentally violate the PCI's design philosophy. The app can provide Carnes' category descriptions and example behaviors as guidance, but the user writes their own items.

2. **Maintain the 12-dimension framework.** The 12 life dimensions provide the structure that ensures users consider their whole life, not just the areas they are already aware of.

3. **Support the 7-item selection.** The reduction from ~36 items to 7 critical items is central to making daily tracking sustainable.

4. **Implement binary daily scoring (0 or 1 per item).** The original PCI uses a simple "did this behavior occur today?" binary, not a rating scale. Some adaptations use intensity scales (1-7 or 0-10), but these are departures from Carnes' original design.

5. **Enforce the "missed day = 7" rule.** If the user does not complete their daily check-in, the day should receive the maximum score. This is a feature, not a punishment -- Carnes explicitly states that inability to track is itself evidence of imbalance.

6. **Provide weekly totaling and 12-week charting.** The weekly aggregation and multi-week trend line are essential for pattern recognition.

7. **Include the 5-level interpretation scale.** The Optimal Health through Very High Risk framework gives meaning to the numbers.

8. **Support item evolution over time.** Users must be able to edit their indicators and critical-7 selections as their recovery progresses. This should not invalidate historical data.

### Practical Mobile UX Considerations

1. **Onboarding: Guided setup.** The initial PCI creation (defining ~36 indicators across 12 dimensions, then selecting 7) is a substantial exercise. This should be:
   - Broken into manageable chunks (one dimension at a time)
   - Presented with Carnes' descriptions and examples for each dimension
   - Completable over multiple sessions (save progress)
   - Ideally preceded by psychoeducation about what the PCI is and why it matters

2. **Daily check-in: Minimal friction.** The daily check-in should be extremely fast -- reviewing 7 items with binary toggles should take under 60 seconds. This positions it as an evening ritual alongside existing app activities.

3. **Weekly summary: Automatic.** The app should automatically calculate weekly totals and display the risk-level interpretation.

4. **Trend visualization.** The paper-based PCI Graph (12-week grid) should be replaced with a dynamic chart showing weekly totals over time, with color-coded risk level bands.

5. **Notification strategy.** The app should prompt for daily PCI check-in (consistent with the existing app's evening activity block). The "missed day = 7" rule creates a natural incentive to check in, but should be communicated clearly during onboarding rather than experienced as a surprise penalty.

6. **Integration with FASTER Scale.** PCI trend data should be visible alongside FASTER Scale check-in data to show the upstream-downstream relationship. A rising PCI trend that precedes a FASTER Scale worsening is a powerful insight.

7. **Integration with Recovery Health Score.** PCI weekly scores should feed into the app's proprietary Recovery Health Score as a component (this is already referenced in the strategic PRD).

### Data Model Implications

A digital PCI requires:

- **PCI Profile:** 12 dimensions, each with 1-4+ user-defined indicators (text strings)
- **Critical 7 Selection:** 7 indicators marked as "critical" from across all dimensions (references into the profile)
- **Daily Check-in:** Date + 7 binary values (one per critical item)
- **Weekly Summary:** Computed weekly total (0-49) + mapped risk level
- **Historical Data:** Full time series of daily check-ins and weekly summaries
- **Profile Versioning:** When users edit their indicators or critical-7 selection, the old version should be retained so historical data remains interpretable

## 11. Notable Adaptations and Variations

Several practitioners have adapted the PCI, which reveals how the tool is being used in practice:

### Transformations Press (David Bookbinder)
- Uses **10 categories** instead of 12 (condensed version)
- Tracks 7 indicators daily with a **reverse scoring** system (7 = doing all positive things, 0 = doing none)
- Provides editable PCI template and tracking spreadsheet downloads
- Frames PCI as a general resilience tool, not addiction-specific

### Mantra of Hope Counselling Services (2025 adaptation)
- Uses a **1-7 intensity scale** per item per day instead of binary 0/1
- Introduces "Routines" and "Behaviours" as the two underlying principles
- Adds a **meditation/journaling step** (LLSRD format: Lies, Lessons, Share, Refine, Do Different)
- Uses 8 domains instead of 12: Physical, Personal relationships (intimate), Personal relationships (general), Spiritual relationships (faith community), Spiritual relationships (fellowship), Work, Finances, Other

### BPB Counseling Group
- Renamed to **"Personal Chaos Index"**
- Used specifically for sex/porn addiction, love addiction, and compulsive sexual behavior recovery

### Augustine Recovery
- Uses a **0-10 intensity scale** per item in a daily diary format
- Suggests tracking over a month or longer
- Recommends noting concurrent events, feelings, meetings, and outreach calls in the diary

## 12. Research Gaps and Uncertainties

### Verified Gaps

1. **No peer-reviewed validation studies found** for the PCI as a psychometric instrument. This is consistent with it being a structured self-awareness exercise rather than a clinical assessment, but it means we cannot cite "evidence-based" support for the tool. [FACT | Medium Confidence]

2. **Exact chapter location in A Gentle Path** is not definitively confirmed. Sources cite p. 243 for the foundational quote, but the full worksheet section location in A Gentle Path (as opposed to Facing the Shadow pp. 278-287) is not independently verified. [PRELIMINARY | Low Confidence]

3. **Original publication context** of the PCI's first appearance -- was it in the 1989 first edition of A Gentle Path, or was it added in a later edition? The claim that it was "adapted" from group home settings for intellectual disability treatment (Dawesome Recovery) could not be corroborated from any second source. [PRELIMINARY | Low Confidence]

4. **Carnes' own recommended frequency** -- while the worksheets describe daily tracking, I could not find Carnes' explicit recommendation for how long a person should use the PCI (indefinitely? First year of recovery? During high-risk periods only?). The interpretation section says "many use it as a daily reminder of their progress" suggesting ongoing use. [PRELIMINARY | Low Confidence]

5. **Recovery Zone book** -- Whether the PCI appears in Carnes' 2009 *Recovery Zone: Making Changes That Last* could not be verified. Given the book's focus on sustaining recovery, it is plausible. [HYPOTHESIS | Low Confidence]

6. **Whether Carnes' scoring uses 0-9 or 1-10 for the lowest tier** -- minor discrepancy between sources in the interpretation table boundary values. The PA Cert Board and Dawesome versions use 0-9 for the lowest tier; the Recovery Behind Bars version uses 1-10. [FACT | Medium Confidence -- discrepancy exists but is minor]
