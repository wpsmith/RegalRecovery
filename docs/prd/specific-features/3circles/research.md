# The three circles: a complete product research brief for building a recovery app

**The Three Circles is the most widely used sobriety-definition tool in sex addiction recovery, yet no purpose-built app adequately digitizes it.** Originating as a 1991 SAA pamphlet, the framework asks individuals to sort behaviors into three concentric rings — inner (addictive), middle (warning), and outer (healthy) — creating a personalized, living recovery blueprint. Its inherent flexibility makes it adaptable across addiction types, from substances to process addictions, but also embeds it in a clinically contested "sex addiction" paradigm that demands careful, inclusive, trauma-informed design. This report synthesizes SAA literature, clinical research, app market analysis, UX best practices, and ethical considerations to give a product team everything needed to implement the three circles well.

---

## How a 1991 pamphlet became the backbone of behavioral addiction recovery

The Three Circles tool was first formally published in **1991** as a pamphlet by Sex Addicts Anonymous titled *"Three Circles: Defining Sobriety in S.A.A."* It emerged collectively from the SAA fellowship — founded in 1977 in Minneapolis — rather than from any single clinical author. While Dr. Patrick Carnes pioneered the broader sex addiction field with *Out of the Shadows* (1983) and later founded IITAP and the CSAT certification, the Three Circles itself is fellowship-developed, not clinician-designed.

The tool was canonized in SAA's *Sex Addicts Anonymous* (the "Green Book"), first published in 2005 with a third edition in 2017, within the chapter "Defining Abstinence." SAA's official description reads: *"To help us define our sexual sobriety, many of us use a tool developed within SAA called The Three Circles. This is only a tool and not a requirement."* The critical innovation is that **SAA does not have a universal definition of abstinence** — unlike AA, where sobriety means zero alcohol, each SAA member defines their own sobriety because "it is not sex, in and of itself, that causes us problems, but the addiction to certain sexual behaviors."

Since 1991, the framework has been adopted far beyond SAA. Dr. Amanda Giordano, in her 2021 *Counseling Today* article and textbook *A Clinical Guide to Treating Behavioral Addictions*, describes the three circles as applicable across gambling, binge eating, social media, and gaming. CSATs routinely integrate it with Carnes' 30-Task recovery model. Internet and Technology Addicts Anonymous (ITAA, founded 2017) and Workaholics Anonymous have explicitly adopted the framework under their own terminology.

The Three Circles maps cleanly onto established relapse prevention theory. **Marlatt and Gordon's cognitive-behavioral relapse prevention model (1985)** provides the closest parallel: high-risk situations map to the middle circle, coping skills and self-efficacy building map to the outer circle, and the framework's distinction between middle-circle behavior (not a sobriety loss) and inner-circle behavior (sobriety loss) directly counters Marlatt's "abstinence violation effect" — the all-or-nothing thinking that turns a lapse into a full relapse. Gorski's CENAPS model shares the concept of escalating warning signs preceding relapse, directly analogous to the middle circle's function. The Stages of Change model (Prochaska & DiClemente) complements the Three Circles by situating the creation of one's circles within the contemplation/preparation stages and ongoing circle use within the action/maintenance stages.

Despite strong theoretical alignment, **no randomized controlled trials exist evaluating the Three Circles as a standalone intervention.** The evidence base is indirect — drawing from well-established evidence for relapse prevention, CBT, and 12-step facilitation — and relies on clinical consensus and participant testimonials rather than empirical validation of the specific tool.

## Inside the three concentric rings: what each circle means and how they interact

### The inner circle defines sobriety

The inner circle contains "the sexual behaviors that we want to stop — the addictive sexual behaviors that brought us to SAA, the things that made us hit bottom in our disease." Engaging in **any** inner-circle behavior constitutes a loss of sobriety, and the member's sobriety date resets to the last day of inner-circle acting out.

SAA provides screening questions to help determine placement: Does the behavior victimize someone? Is it life- or health-threatening? Is it illegal? Does it damage relationships, finances, employment, self-esteem, or spirituality? Common inner-circle examples for sex/porn addiction include viewing pornography, compulsive masturbation (when identified as addictive), anonymous sex or hookups, affairs, commercial sex, cybersex, and cruising for sexual encounters.

The Green Book warns against both extremes: *"If we are too lenient with ourselves, we might not get sober. If we are too strict, we might restrict ourselves from healthy behaviors that we have no need to give up, and an inability to meet our high standards could set us up for relapse."* The Green Book Step Study recommends putting **as few items as possible** in the inner circle initially, concentrating on the most destructive behaviors first — a counterintuitive but clinically pragmatic approach.

### The middle circle catches you before you fall

The middle circle is the framework's most sophisticated element and, according to clinicians, the most commonly underestimated. SAA describes it as behaviors that are "slippery for us or about which we are uncertain." Critically, *"if we engage in middle-circle behavior, we have not lost our sobriety, but it is a signal that we need to reach out to others."*

The middle circle functions as **a safety net, not a punishment zone** — "allowing us to walk the tightrope of abstinence without having to fear that a false step would necessarily be disastrous." It contains behavioral precursors and rituals (contacting an old partner, staying up late on the computer), emotional triggers captured by the **HALT+B** acronym (Hungry, Angry, Lonely, Tired, Bored), uncertain behaviors the person hasn't yet determined are addictive, environmental triggers, and lifestyle warning signs like overwork, isolation, or skipping meetings.

Carol Juergensen Sheets, LCSW, CSAT at The Meadows, identifies underestimating middle-circle behaviors as the single most dangerous pitfall: *"There's a lot of denial with middle circle behaviors because addicts believe they can engage in some behaviors that aren't good for them yet stay away from 'real addictive behaviors.' This is a misnomer as invariably, the addict who dabbles in unhealthy behaviors will eventually be drawn to severe addictive relapse."*

The prescribed response when entering the middle circle is active, not passive: go to a meeting and discuss the activity, or call someone in the program. SAA experience indicates that less active responses (silent prayer, telling a partner alone) "have not helped our recovery very much."

### The outer circle is where recovery lives

The outer circle contains "behaviors we consider healthy, safe, and beneficial to our recovery" — acts of self-nurturing that bring meaning, fulfillment, serenity, and joy. It explicitly includes healthy sexuality: dating, non-abusive sex within a committed relationship, healthy masturbation, and appropriate physical affection. It also encompasses recovery activities (meetings, sponsor calls, step work), physical self-care (exercise, sleep, nutrition), emotional care (therapy, meditation), social connection (family, friendships, service), and creative/recreational pursuits.

A key clinical insight from Restored Hope Counseling: *"Choosing to practice healthy self-care will literally help to re-wire your brain to reduce cravings and replace desire to act out with other enjoyable activities."* The outer circle is deliberately the largest ring in the diagram — users can never have too many outer-circle behaviors.

### The dynamics between circles: a traffic light and a slope

The circles interact through a gravitational metaphor: **the natural slope of addiction pulls inward.** When an individual neglects outer-circle behaviors and drifts into middle-circle territory, progression toward the inner circle accelerates. Recovery means moving outward. Many groups use a traffic-light analogy: inner = red (STOP), middle = yellow (CAUTION), outer = green (GO). Some SAA groups add a "middle bubble" — a fourth zone between middle and inner for behaviors that, while not technically inner-circle, will almost certainly lead there.

Every circle is personalized by the individual with sponsor or therapist guidance, and every circle evolves. The SAA Green Book states explicitly that circles are "not permanently set for all time" but warns against changing them without sponsor consultation. Michael Gargano, LMHC, who conducted his entire sponsorship relationship via phone and email, describes how his circles evolved as he addressed underlying trauma: *"Recovery is not stagnant. As I grew in healthiness, sexuality took on a richer meaning for me."*

## How people actually use the three circles day to day

### Creating the circles: worksheets, sponsors, and therapists

Multiple structured resources exist for initial circle creation. The Houston SAA Intergroup publishes a widely-used downloadable PDF worksheet with structured fields for each circle, including action plans, support contacts, and start dates. The Central Florida SAA provides a summary sheet compiling Green Book questions. Restoration Soul Care offers a printable PDF with a companion YouTube instructional video. Oak Mountain Coaching provides a color-coded worksheet (red/yellow/green) with sample completed circles.

Clinicians recommend a specific creation sequence. Restored Hope Counseling advises starting with the inner circle (the abstinence list), then the outer circle (self-care and healthy behaviors), saving the middle circle for last because it "can be the most complex" and requires observing one's own addiction cycle. Robert Weiss of Seeking Integrity recommends beginning with a statement of goals before categorizing behaviors and emphasizes listing specific behaviors, not thoughts or fantasies. Dr. Paul Schmidt expands the model to four circles and requires "very specific" behavioral descriptions "to minimize confusion about whether you have done something."

Sponsors play a foundational role. SAA explicitly states circles are created "with the help of our sponsor or others in recovery" and warns that defining recovery in isolation may result in self-deceptively loose or punishingly strict definitions. Gargano's experience demonstrates that effective sponsorship around the three circles can happen entirely remotely — a key insight for app design.

### Daily rhythms: check-ins, calls, and journaling

Active users follow a morning/evening rhythm: morning intention-setting and evening reflection journaling. Daily phone calls with accountability partners are standard practice. The Restoration Soul Care framework uses a **S.E.E.D.S. daily tracker** mapping outer-circle engagement across Social/Spiritual, Education, Exercise, Diet, and Sleep domains. Weekly reviews — typically Sunday or before a group meeting — involve reflecting on circle drift, assessing outer-circle engagement, and adjusting plans.

Dr. Schmidt's reporting structure provides an instructive model for app design: report on sobriety status first (inner circle), then recovery behaviors (outer circle), then slips (middle circle), then triggers and attitudes. Reports go to spouse, sponsor, therapist, and same-sex accountability partners — with different levels of detail for each relationship.

### When things go wrong: slips, relapses, and the reset

A slip is defined as a single, unplanned breach of an inner-circle behavior; a relapse is a conscious, intentional return to acting out or an abandoned recovery plan. Three criteria distinguish them: **duration** (quick or extended), **repetition** (one-time or pattern), and **premeditation** (spontaneous or planned). Any inner-circle behavior resets the sobriety clock — though an alternative perspective from Bradford Health argues "you can never take away a sober day" and recommends honoring both the pre-slip and post-slip sobriety periods.

After a slip, the recommended protocol intensifies recovery work: add one extra weekly 12-step meeting for 12 weeks, add one extra daily recovery call, build a "circle of five" accountability contacts, and revisit circle definitions with a sponsor. Warning signs of impending relapse include overconfidence, retreat from the support network, defensiveness, resumption of old behaviors, and abandoning step work. Particularly vulnerable periods occur around **6 months, 18 months, and 3 years** of recovery.

### Six mistakes that undermine the framework

The most commonly cited pitfalls across clinical and community sources reveal critical design implications. **Vague definitions** invite rationalization — Siam Rehab notes that "precise boundaries create faster recognition and response." **Excessive rigidity** creates impossible standards and sets up shame spirals when they inevitably fail. **Shame-based framing** turns a growth tool into a punishment mechanism, reinforcing the isolation-secrecy-shame cycle that fuels addiction. **Underestimating the middle circle** — the single most emphasized pitfall — leads users to believe they can dabble in boundary behaviors without progressing to acting out. **Changing circles out of convenience** rather than through honest conversation with a sponsor undermines the tool's integrity. And **defining circles in isolation** without sponsor or group input leads to either self-deceptively loose or punishingly strict boundaries.

## Adapting the framework across addiction types: where it fits and where it stretches

The Three Circles translates most naturally to **process addictions** — behavioral patterns where total abstinence from the underlying activity is impossible or undesirable. It was designed precisely for this challenge: you cannot abstain from sex or intimacy entirely, so you need a nuanced framework that distinguishes healthy from unhealthy expressions.

**SLAA** (Sex and Love Addicts Anonymous) uses functionally identical "bottom lines / middle lines / top lines" terminology. **ITAA** (Internet and Technology Addicts Anonymous, founded 2017) has explicitly adopted the three circles, acknowledging the unique challenge that "because technology is a part of our everyday lives, sobriety in ITAA is a process of discovery." The reSTART addiction treatment center, recognized as the nation's first internet gaming addiction treatment center, formally uses ITAA's three circles model in clinical treatment. **Workaholics Anonymous** uses bottom lines and top lines, with particularly insightful framing: *"It is often the motives, attitudes, and energy that we bring to a particular situation that render our behavior compulsive rather than the nature of the activity itself."*

**Overeaters Anonymous** uses a functionally analogous "Plan of Eating" system rather than three-circles terminology. Food Addicts in Recovery Anonymous (FA) takes a stricter unified approach: no flour, sugar, or sweeteners, with weighed and measured meals. The food addiction context highlights the framework's central tension — you cannot abstain from eating, so the inner circle must target specific behaviors (binging, purging, eating trigger foods) rather than the substance itself. **Debtors Anonymous** implicitly mirrors the three circles: abstinence means no new unsecured debt (inner circle), spending plans serve as middle/outer circle management, and "Pressure Relief Groups" function like sponsors for financial behavior.

For **substance addictions** (alcohol, drugs), the inner circle is simpler — abstain from the substance — making the middle and outer circles relatively more important for relapse prevention. AA and NA do not formally use the framework, but treatment centers like The Meadows and Siam Rehab apply it with substance abuse clients, using the middle circle for emotional states and situations (going to bars "just to socialize," isolation, overwork) and the outer circle for meetings, exercise, and healthy relationships. **Gamblers Anonymous** uses a universal abstinence model without three-circles formalization, though Harry Mayr independently developed a concentric-circles concept for gambling counseling in 1982.

| Addiction type | Abstinence approach | Three circles fit |
|---|---|---|
| Sex/porn (SAA) | Abstain from specific behaviors, not sex itself | Designed for this — ideal fit |
| Technology/internet (ITAA) | Can't abstain from all technology | Essential — closest parallel to SAA |
| Work (WA) | Can't abstain from all work | Essential — motives matter more than activity |
| Food (OA/FA) | Can't abstain from food; abstain from trigger behaviors | Highly useful but requires careful framing |
| Love/relationships (SLAA) | Can't abstain from all relationships | Direct adoption with identical terminology |
| Spending/debt (DA) | Abstain from new unsecured debt, not all spending | Well-suited — implicit adoption |
| Alcohol/drugs (AA/NA) | Total substance abstinence | Middle/outer circles add value; inner circle simpler |
| Gambling (GA) | Total gambling abstinence | Less essential but applicable |
| Codependency (CoDA) | Change relational patterns, not abstain from relationships | Adaptable but not formally adopted |

## The app landscape: what exists, what works, and what's missing

### The current field of recovery apps

**Relay** is the most notable app implementing the Three Circles digitally. Users define their own recovery goals through the three-circles tracking system, with customizable inner/middle/outer circle activities, daily "pulse checks," a "red flag" panic button that notifies their accountability team, and a 16-week structured program with video lessons from licensed therapists. Relay has earned **4.8 stars** on both app stores with over 122,000 downloads. It's Y Combinator-backed and offers both secular and faith-focused tracks.

**12StepConnect** provides an explicit digital Three Circles tool for SAA users alongside digital inventories, daily meditations per fellowship, and real-time sponsor/sponsee progress sharing via private codes. One user reported: *"The Three Circles tool for SAA is exactly what I needed."*

Beyond explicit three-circles implementations, the market fragments into categories. **Education-focused apps** like Fortify (by Fight the New Drug) offer 52 video lessons across 15 learning paths with analytics, used by ~80,000+ people in 155 countries, but no real-time urge support or content blocking. **Gamified recovery apps** like Brainbuddy feature a 100-day neuroscience-based program with a "Life Tree" visualization that grows with positive choices, though users report persistent bugs and hidden paywalls (**4.5–4.6 stars**, 27,000+ reviews). **Blocker-first apps** like Remojo and Covenant Eyes prioritize content filtering and device monitoring — Covenant Eyes has operated for 20+ years using AI-powered screenshot analysis — but lack recovery education or community features. **Monitoring apps** like Accountable2You track browsing, app usage, and even text messages with real-time alerts to partners, but feel surveillance-oriented rather than recovery-oriented.

### What users consistently want and don't get

User feedback across app stores, Reddit communities (r/pornfree, r/SexAddiction, r/NoFap), and recovery forums reveals consistent patterns. Users praise community and group features above all else, followed by streak tracking, science-based educational content, anonymous support that reduces shame barriers, and panic buttons for acute urges. The most common complaints center on cost (especially hidden paywalls), buggy implementations, non-responsive support teams, and the fragmented nature of solutions — users routinely need **2–3 apps** to cover blocking, tracking, education, and community needs.

### Ten gaps defining the market opportunity

The research reveals a significant market gap for a purpose-built, well-designed digital Three Circles tool. **No standalone app** makes the three-circles exercise fully interactive and dynamic — Relay embeds it within a group-based program, and 12StepConnect includes it as one feature among many. No app offers rich interactive visualization where users drag and drop behaviors between circles and track how their circles evolve over time. No app connects daily check-in data to circle-specific behavior patterns. No app enables real-time collaborative circle-editing between sponsor and sponsee. Most apps track binary outcomes (clean day vs. relapse) without capturing the nuanced emotional states driving middle-circle drift. A **2022 PMC systematic analysis of 170 pornography-related apps** found that only 12.9% provided variable tracking, only 15.9% had diary features, and only 2.9% included any assessment tool. No app reviewed contained references to literature providing direct evidence for the app's efficacy or safety.

Additional underserved areas include partner/betrayed spouse support (almost no app addresses this), women and LGBTQ+ users (most apps target heterosexual men with masculine marketing and language), offline crisis functionality, and long-term recovery maintenance beyond the initial 90-day "quit" phase.

## Designing the ideal three circles app: features, UX, and trauma-informed principles

### Onboarding: guided circle creation with templates, not prescriptions

Walk users through one circle at a time, starting with the inner circle (hardest emotionally but most concrete), then the outer circle (encouraging and expansive), then the middle circle (most nuanced, benefits from reflection on the first two). Provide optional **addiction-specific templates** — pre-populated example behaviors for sex/porn, substances, gambling, food, technology, and other categories that users can accept, modify, or discard. Emphasize personalization: the app suggests, the user decides. Include a warm, trauma-informed introduction acknowledging that this process requires confronting uncomfortable truths. Establish privacy and trust immediately by explaining data handling before requesting any sensitive input. Allow multiple circle sets for users managing co-occurring addictions.

### Daily check-ins: under 60 seconds, branching to depth

Research from mood-tracking apps (Daylio, MindDoc, Bearable) and clinical mHealth studies shows optimal check-ins take less than 60 seconds with optional deeper engagement. A recommended daily flow: (1) "Which circle have you been living in today?" via a visual circle selector; (2) mood/emotional state via a 5-point emoji scale; (3) specific behaviors from the user's defined lists via checkboxes; (4) urge/craving intensity via a 0–10 slider; (5) optional triggers, context, sleep quality, social connection notes; (6) "What's one outer-circle thing you did today?" for positive reinforcement. After check-in, branch to choices: reflect in journal, view patterns, access coping tools, or close — always respecting user autonomy.

### Visualization: interactive rings, time-series patterns, and insight cards

The primary visualization should be the three concentric rings, color-coded (red/yellow/green), tappable to reveal defined behaviors within each. A dashboard view shows current status, recent check-in data, and mood trends. Time-series visualizations display which circle the user has been living in over weeks and months using color bands. **Pattern insight cards** highlight correlations: "You tend to move toward middle circle on Fridays" or "Connecting with your sponsor correlates with outer-circle days." Critically, avoid leaderboards, social comparison, or any competitive gamification.

### Handling slips and relapses with compassion, not shame

Never use shame language. Replace "You failed" or "streak lost" with "Recovery includes setbacks. Let's understand what happened and plan your next step." Frame slips as data points for pattern analysis, not punitive marks. Offer immediate support options: call sponsor, guided urge surfing exercise, grounding techniques, journaling prompt focused on what middle-circle behaviors preceded the event. Track "sobriety time" but consider also showing cumulative recovery days — "You've been in active recovery for 347 days, including 12 days of learning through setbacks." Use growing metaphors (garden, tree rings) rather than sterile counters.

### Accountability that respects privacy and autonomy

Research by Sam Liberty (UbiComp 2025) found that **social mechanics ranked dead last** among desired features for clinical populations — users want to consume community knowledge but not expose their own health struggles. Design accordingly: let users choose exactly what to share (high-level status without behavioral details), offer one-way visibility options, implement granular per-feature per-person sharing controls, and make all sharing opt-in with easy revocation. For therapist integration, generate exportable session summaries (mood trends, circle activity, urge patterns) and offer opt-in shared dashboards with agreed-upon metrics only. If the app transmits protected health information to a covered entity, Business Associate Agreements and HIPAA compliance become necessary.

### Privacy as a first-class feature

Addiction recovery data — especially sexual behavior tracking — is among the most sensitive health data possible. The app should offer **disguised app icons** (calculator, notes, weather), alternative app names on the home screen, stealth notifications with generic text that doesn't reveal recovery context, biometric locks with optional duress PINs showing decoy content, and quick-hide panic buttons. Store sensitive behavior definitions and logs on-device whenever possible with end-to-end encryption for any cloud storage. Adopt HIPAA-level security regardless of legal requirement. Include no third-party tracking or advertising SDKs. A Finnish psychotherapy app hack that led to blackmail of thousands of users demonstrates the catastrophic consequences of security failures in this space.

### Trauma-informed design grounded in SAMHSA's six principles

A 2025 scoping review found that **99% of 117 digital health studies did not explicitly use trauma-informed principles** — a massive gap this app can address. SAMHSA's six principles translate directly to design decisions. **Safety** means robust privacy, predictable navigation, and no surprise content changes. **Trustworthiness** means transparent data policies in plain language and consistent UI behavior. **Peer support** means optional, anonymous community access with no forced sharing. **Collaboration** means users control their own circles — the app never prescribes diagnoses or behaviors. **Empowerment** means users set their own goals, pace, and notification preferences with no mandatory engagement. **Cultural sensitivity** means customizable language, recognition of diverse backgrounds, and avoiding culturally specific metaphors.

Specific language guidelines: avoid "addict," "abuser," "clean/dirty," "failed," and "weakness." Use person-first language ("person in recovery"), compassionate framing ("this is part of the journey"), and normalizing language ("many people experience this"). Error states should frame mistakes as normal and recoverable. Provide content warnings before potentially triggering reflections, "take a break" prompts during intense journaling, and exit options at any time without losing progress.

### Crisis and urge handling: the 15-minute wave

Urges typically pass in 15–30 minutes. A one-tap "I'm having an urge" button accessible from any screen should launch a guided urge surfing exercise based on Dr. Alan Marlatt's Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention: notice and name the urge, rate intensity on a 0–10 slider, guided box breathing (inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4), body scan awareness, visualization of the urge as a wave, re-rate intensity after riding the wave, and a journal prompt capturing what was learned. Include a timer showing urge duration to reinforce that urges pass. Supplement with **5-4-3-2-1 sensory grounding** exercises, temperature-shift prompts, and movement resets. Integrate one-tap access to the **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**, SAMHSA helpline (1-800-662-4357), and custom emergency contacts. Include a built-in, personalized safety plan feature and a location-based meeting finder for SAA, AA, NA, and SMART Recovery meetings.

### Notifications: non-specific, user-controlled, never shaming

Delay notification permission requests until users have explored the app. Default to one daily check-in reminder at a user-selected time. All notification text should be vague enough to protect privacy if seen by others — "Time for your daily reflection" rather than "Recovery check-in." If pattern detection shows higher-risk times (late nights, weekends), offer proactive support: "It's Saturday evening. Would you like to review your coping tools?" Include a stealth mode where all notifications appear as generic calendar or wellness reminders. Support quick-action notifications that allow logging a check-in directly from the notification shade.

### Making it work across addictions

Use an addiction-agnostic core framework with customizable templates. During onboarding, ask "What area of recovery are you working on?" with options spanning sexual behaviors, substances, gambling, food/eating, internet/pornography, process addictions, and custom categories. Provide template starter kits with pre-populated example behaviors per addiction type. The UI, check-ins, and visualizations remain identical across addiction types — only user-defined content changes. Adapt vocabulary based on selected recovery type ("sobriety" vs. "abstinence" vs. "recovery"). Allow multiple circle sets for co-occurring addictions, since cross-addiction is common.

### What the research says about digital recovery effectiveness

A **JMIR 2026 study** of a co-designed mHealth recovery app showed **45.8% 30-day retention** among active users, versus a 7% industry benchmark — co-design with end users was the key differentiator. The SURE Recovery app study (JMIR 2022) found that how an app makes users *feel* matters as much as behavioral outcomes, and that incorporating credible organizational partners increases uptake. The Seva mHealth system (JMIR 2018) demonstrated that real-time peer support via discussion boards — even at 4:41 AM — was transformative for sustained use. Across studies, effects are minimal unless the app is used in conjunction with other treatments. The strongest finding: **apps work best as complements to, not replacements for, professional treatment and human connection.**

On gamification, an MDPI 2025 analysis identified an **engagement-efficacy-ethics trilemma**: increased engagement does not consistently improve therapeutic outcomes, and maximizing engagement can introduce manipulation and psychological distress. Sam Liberty's field research found that achievement systems feel patronizing to clinical populations, opaque reward systems make users feel manipulated, and variable reward schedules mirror the very addictive patterns users are trying to escape. The recommendation: use "conscious gamification" — transparent, user-controlled, aligned with long-term recovery goals, with no leaderboards, no social comparison, and no loss-aversion mechanics.

## The contested clinical landscape: what a product team must understand

### Community-validated, not clinically validated

The Three Circles is **a community-developed self-help exercise, not an evidence-based clinical intervention.** No RCTs, no systematic reviews, and no psychometric validation exist for the tool specifically. Gold and Heffner's 1998 landmark review in *Clinical Psychological Review* concluded that the sex addiction literature "consists largely of theory and conjecture based almost entirely on clinical observation rather than on research findings." Grubbs et al.'s 2020 systematic review confirmed that significant methodological gaps persist.

The tool does have **face validity** and strong **clinical consensus** — CSATs, addiction counselors, and treatment centers like The Meadows use it routinely. Its underlying principles (trigger identification, behavioral categorization, healthy replacement behaviors) are well-established in cognitive-behavioral relapse prevention research. But the specific tool remains practice-based rather than empirically validated.

### The diagnosis debate matters for app language

The framing of problematic sexual behavior is actively contested across three major positions. The **pro-addiction model** (Carnes, IITAP, SASH) treats sex addiction as a brain disease requiring 12-step recovery. The **anti-addiction model** (Ley, Prause, AASECT) argues the addiction label pathologizes normal sexual variation, removes moral agency, and is not supported by neuroscience — Prause's EEG studies found brain responses in self-identified hypersexual individuals reflected high desire rather than addiction-like patterns. The **middle ground** (WHO's ICD-11) includes **Compulsive Sexual Behavior Disorder (CSBD)** as an impulse control disorder, explicitly *not* classified as an addiction, with a crucial exclusion: *"Distress that is entirely related to moral judgments and disapproval about sexual impulses, urges, or behaviours is not sufficient."*

The **DSM-5 rejected sex addiction** as a diagnosis entirely, stating that "to include this as an addiction would require published scientific research that does not exist at this time." AASECT's 2016 position statement declared that it "does not find sufficient empirical evidence to support the classification of sex addiction or porn addiction as a mental health disorder."

For the app, this means language choices carry weight. "Addiction" implies a brain disease model and carries stigma. "Compulsive behavior" aligns with ICD-11 and is less stigmatizing. "Problematic behavior" is the most neutral and client-centered. Grubbs' longitudinal research shows that **self-identification as a "sex/porn addict" actually predicts greater life problems, regardless of actual use frequency** — suggesting the label itself can be harmful. The app should default to neutral language while allowing users to choose their own framing.

### LGBTQ+ users face unique risks from this framework

This may be the most critical ethical consideration. David Ley documents that **gay and bisexual men are three times more likely to be labeled sex addicts**, and some clinicians have used the addiction model as a form of conversion therapy — rebranding homosexuality as sex addiction to circumvent conversion therapy bans. Jennings, Gleason, and Kraus published peer-reviewed recommendations (2022, 2024) emphasizing that clinicians must distinguish between minority stress-related distress and genuine sexual compulsivity, using minority stress theory as a clinical framework.

Without careful framing, Three Circles users may place same-sex behaviors, BDSM/kink, or consensual non-monogamy in the inner circle based on internalized homophobia or cultural pressure rather than genuine dysfunction. The app must explicitly validate sexual diversity, state that sexual orientation and consensual practices are not disorders, include LGBTQ+-affirming content, and screen for internalized minority stress versus genuine compulsivity.

### Moral incongruence: when the problem isn't the behavior

Joshua Grubbs' research program on **moral incongruence** has demonstrated that perceived pornography addiction is strongly related to moral disapproval of pornography — independent of actual use levels. Religiosity and moral disapproval are more robust predictors of self-perceived addiction than frequency of use. This has direct implications for the Three Circles: users may place behaviors in the inner circle based on moral or religious conviction rather than genuine dysfunction. The app could inadvertently amplify moral incongruence by providing a framework that validates culturally-driven distress as clinical addiction.

The responsible design response is to include psychoeducational content about moral incongruence, normalize the distinction between values-based discomfort and clinical dysfunction, and actively nudge users toward professional assessment — particularly when self-defined circles appear driven by shame rather than harm.

### The app must complement, never replace, human care

Harvard Health research confirms that **the strongest factor in helping patients make progress is the relationship with the therapist** — no app can replicate therapeutic alliance. The app should include prominent messaging that it is not a substitute for professional treatment, integrate pathways to human support (therapist directories, sponsor contacts, meeting finders) throughout the experience, and actively recommend professional help when users report suicidal ideation, self-harm, legal-risk behaviors, underlying mental health conditions, relationship distress, or persistent failure of self-defined recovery plans.

Data privacy considerations are especially acute for addiction apps. The Legal Action Center warns that "personal data associated with seeking or receiving addiction treatment may lead to criminal prosecution, loss of child custody, discrimination, eviction, and denial of healthcare." Most consumer health apps are not covered entities under HIPAA, meaning data can legally be shared, sold, or subpoenaed. ExpressVPN's Digital Security Lab found that opioid recovery apps commonly share data with third parties without full disclosure. The FTC levied a **$7.8 million penalty** against Cerebral in 2024 for sharing protected health information with advertisers. The app should adopt the strongest possible data protection regardless of legal requirements: end-to-end encryption, local-first storage, zero third-party data sharing, transparent privacy policies, and clear deletion mechanisms.

## Conclusion: building for recovery, not surveillance

The Three Circles framework succeeds because it respects individual autonomy — each person defines their own sobriety, their own warning signs, and their own healthy life. The most effective digital implementation will preserve this autonomy while adding what paper worksheets cannot: pattern recognition across time, instant connection to support during urges, evolving visualizations of growth, and frictionless accountability that doesn't require surveillance.

The critical design tensions to navigate are precision versus compassion (clear behavioral definitions without shame-based rigidity), structure versus autonomy (guided frameworks without prescriptive diagnoses), engagement versus ethics (meaningful interaction without addictive gamification patterns), and privacy versus accountability (transparency with trusted supporters without exposing sensitive data to exploitation).

The market opportunity is clear and specific: **no app currently provides a purpose-built, interactive, evolving Three Circles tool** with pattern analysis, sponsor collaboration, cross-addiction templates, and trauma-informed design. The closest competitors embed the framework within broader programs (Relay) or offer it as one feature among many (12StepConnect). A dedicated Three Circles app — designed with clinical humility about the framework's contested evidence base, genuine inclusivity for diverse populations, and relentless commitment to user safety — would fill a gap that hundreds of thousands of people in recovery are currently navigating with paper worksheets, scattered app combinations, and sheer willpower.

The three most consequential decisions for the product team: adopt neutral, non-pathologizing default language while letting users choose their own framing; invest heavily in privacy architecture because breach consequences in this domain are catastrophic; and co-design with actual recovery communities across addiction types, backgrounds, and identities — because the research is unequivocal that co-designed mHealth interventions outperform expert-designed ones by an order of magnitude in retention and engagement.
