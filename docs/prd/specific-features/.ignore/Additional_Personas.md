# Additional User Personas

These personas address representation gaps in the original PRD. The existing personas skew heavily toward married, Texas-based, evangelical users. These additions ensure the app's design accounts for single users without spousal dynamics, and Spanish-speaking users whose cultural and linguistic context shapes their recovery experience.

---

## Persona: "Addict Marcus" — Single Male

### Demographics

- Age: 27
- Occupation: Electrician (union apprentice)
- Location: Charlotte, NC
- Marital Status: Single, never married; ended a 3-year relationship 8 months ago due to his addiction
- Faith: Non-denominational Christian, attends a small church plant irregularly
- Recovery Stage: Early recovery (21 days sober)
- Living Situation: Lives alone in a one-bedroom apartment

### Background

Marcus has struggled with pornography since age 13 and compulsive sexual behavior since college. His ex-girlfriend Keisha ended their relationship after discovering the extent of his addiction — a loss that finally pushed him to seek help. He started attending a Celebrate Recovery group at a church across town (not his own church — he's too ashamed to go to his home church's group). He doesn't have a sponsor yet and isn't seeing a therapist due to cost. His primary support is his CR group and his older brother Darren, who knows about the addiction but doesn't fully understand it.

Marcus's recovery is complicated by the fact that he lives alone with no built-in accountability structure. Evenings and weekends are his most dangerous times. Without a spouse or family at home, there's no external check on his behavior — if he relapses, no one knows unless he tells them. His social life has shrunk since the breakup, and most of his friends still consume pornography casually, which makes it hard to talk about his struggle without being dismissed.

### Goals

- Build a recovery life without a partner to hold him accountable — learn to be accountable to himself and God
- Find a sponsor who understands what it's like to be single in recovery
- Develop a healthy social life that doesn't revolve around dating, drinking, or digital consumption
- Process the grief of losing Keisha without using it as an excuse to relapse
- Afford professional help (therapy, recovery resources) on an apprentice's income
- Eventually become someone capable of a healthy relationship — but not rush into one

### Pain Points

- **No Built-In Accountability:** Lives alone — no spouse, no roommate, no one to notice if he disappears into his addiction for a weekend
- **Evening & Weekend Vulnerability:** Long, unstructured hours alone in his apartment are his highest-risk periods; the app needs to be especially useful during these windows
- **Financial Barriers:** Can't afford weekly therapy ($150/session); needs the app to fill the gap between free CR meetings and professional help he can't access
- **Social Isolation:** Lost his girlfriend and most of his social circle; rebuilding community from scratch while sober is daunting
- **Stigma Among Peers:** His male friends normalize pornography; talking about addiction feels like overreacting to them
- **No Spousal Features Needed — But Feels Excluded:** Much of the recovery content he encounters (apps, books, groups) assumes he's married and focuses on rebuilding trust with a spouse — he needs content for single men rebuilding themselves
- **Sponsor Gap:** Hasn't found a sponsor; nervous about asking someone; needs the app to provide some of the structure a sponsor would until he finds one
- **Dating Temptation:** Struggles with the urge to use dating apps as a substitute for genuine connection; needs tools to recognize when he's acting out relationally, not just sexually

### Technology Behaviors

- Uses Samsung Galaxy A54 (mid-range Android)
- Heavy smartphone user — phone is his primary entertainment device, which is also his primary trigger device
- Uses YouTube, Reddit, and Twitch extensively
- Comfortable with apps but won't pay for them unless he sees immediate value
- Values simplicity — will stop using anything that feels like extra work after a long shift

### Quote

*"Everyone talks about rebuilding your marriage. I don't have a marriage. I barely have a friend group anymore. I need help figuring out how to be a whole person on my own before I can even think about being with someone else."*

### App Design Implications

- **Content personalization for single users:** Motivations, affirmations, and devotionals should include options that don't assume a spouse exists (e.g., "Live with integrity" rather than "Rebuild trust with your spouse" as a default)
- **Evening/weekend engagement:** The app should offer heightened engagement options during high-risk hours — extended devotionals, guided activities, community connection features, or "accountability check" prompts
- **Affordable premium path:** The freemium tier must deliver real value — Marcus won't convert to premium unless the free experience convinces him the paid content is worth sacrificing other spending
- **Sponsor-lite features:** For users without a sponsor, the app should provide structure that partially fills the role — daily accountability prompts, step work guidance, and a path to finding a sponsor through the community
- **Friendship and community beyond couples:** Community features should support connection between single users in recovery, not just couple-to-couple or addict-to-spouse dynamics
- **Device as trigger awareness:** Content or tools that help Marcus establish healthy phone boundaries (screen time awareness, trigger app identification, "safe browsing" reminders) without requiring a separate accountability software

## Persona: "Addict Jasmine" — Single Female

### Demographics

- Age: 24
- Occupation: Graduate student (MSW — Master of Social Work)
- Location: Nashville, TN
- Marital Status: Single, dating casually (which is part of the problem)
- Faith: AME (African Methodist Episcopal), raised in the church, currently questioning but still attending
- Recovery Stage: Pre-recovery / early contemplation (3 days since she decided to get help)
- Living Situation: Lives with two roommates who don't know about her addiction

### Background

Jasmine has struggled with compulsive sexual behavior and love addiction for 5 years. Her pattern involves cycling through intense, short-lived sexual relationships and using dating apps compulsively — swiping, matching, and seeking validation through sexual attention. She recognized it as an addiction 6 months ago during a psychology class on behavioral addictions, but it took a pregnancy scare and a particularly degrading encounter to push her toward help.

As a young Black woman in the church, Jasmine faces layers of stigma. Her church culture doesn't openly discuss sex addiction — it barely discusses sex at all. She's terrified of being judged as promiscuous rather than understood as addicted. She's also studying to be a social worker, which makes her acutely aware of the clinical dimensions of her behavior but also makes her feel like she "should know better," compounding her shame.

She found Regal Recovery through a Reddit thread. She hasn't told anyone about her addiction — not her roommates, not her mother, not her pastor. She's considering finding a therapist but hasn't made the call yet.

### Goals

- Acknowledge and name her addiction without being crushed by shame
- Find other women — especially women of color — who understand compulsive sexual behavior and love addiction
- Develop healthy relationship patterns and learn to be alone without seeking validation through sexual attention
- Reconcile her faith with her behavior — she feels like a hypocrite sitting in church on Sunday after her Saturday nights
- Build a recovery plan she can follow while managing graduate school, clinical placements, and a social life
- Eventually tell someone she trusts — but she's not ready yet and needs the app to be her first safe space

### Pain Points

- **Total Secrecy:** No one in her life knows. Her roommates, classmates, church family, and mother all see a high-achieving, faithful young woman. The gap between her public image and private behavior is destroying her from the inside.
- **Love Addiction Overlap:** Her addiction isn't just about sex — it's about the pursuit of romantic intensity, the validation of being desired, and the inability to tolerate emotional aloneness. Most sex addiction tools focus on pornography and don't address love/relationship addiction
- **Cultural & Racial Isolation:** Almost no recovery resources speak to her experience as a young Black woman. Recovery spaces are predominantly white and male. She needs to see herself reflected somewhere.
- **Church Silence:** Her church doesn't talk about sex addiction. Purity culture messaging makes her feel dirty, not helped. She needs faith-integrated content that offers grace, not guilt.
- **Dating App Compulsion:** Her phone is her primary acting-out tool — not through pornography but through dating apps (Hinge, Tinder, Bumble). She needs tools that address digital relationship-seeking as an addictive behavior
- **Graduate School Stress:** Intense academic pressure and emotional labor from clinical placements create constant stress — a primary trigger for her acting-out cycle
- **Age & Stage:** At 24, she's younger than most recovery personas. Her social world revolves around dating, nightlife, and social media — environments saturated with triggers. Recovery advice that assumes a settled, married adult doesn't translate to her life.
- **Professional Identity Conflict:** She's studying to help others with mental health challenges while secretly struggling with her own. The shame of being a "wounded healer" before she's even licensed is paralyzing.

### Technology Behaviors

- Uses iPhone 15
- Heavy social media user — Instagram, TikTok, Twitter/X, dating apps
- Expects polished, modern UX — will judge the app by its design quality within seconds
- Uses her phone as her journal (Notes app) and therapist (Reddit lurking)
- Values anonymity features above all else — will not use any feature that could expose her
- Consumes content via audio (podcasts, audiobooks) during commutes and workouts

### Quote

*"I know what this is clinically. I can name the attachment wounds, the dopamine cycle, all of it. But knowing doesn't stop me from doing it. I need something that helps me in the moment — at 11 PM on a Friday when I'm about to open Hinge again — not another article about brain chemistry."*

### App Design Implications

- **Love addiction and relationship addiction content:** The app should address compulsive relationship-seeking, validation addiction, and emotional dependency — not just pornography and physical acting out. Affirmations, journal prompts, and check-in questions should include these dimensions.
- **Anonymous-first experience:** Jasmine needs to feel completely safe before she'll engage. The app should function fully without connecting to any support network. No pressure to add a sponsor, no prompts to "share with your accountability partner" before she's ready.
- **Representation in content:** Devotionals, affirmations, and imagery should reflect diverse racial and ethnic backgrounds. Recovery stories and testimonials should include women of color. This doesn't mean separate content — it means inclusive content.
- **Dating app trigger tools:** The urge logging and emergency tools should include dating app compulsion as a recognized trigger category. Coping strategies should address the specific pattern of swipe → match → message → meet → regret.
- **Audio-first content options:** Devotionals, affirmations, and prayers with TTS or recorded audio support her preferred content consumption mode
- **Younger user UX expectations:** The app's design quality, animation polish, and interaction patterns need to meet the standard set by the consumer apps she uses daily (Instagram, TikTok). A dated or clinical-looking UI will be dismissed immediately.
- **Pre-support-network value:** The app must deliver its full value for a user who has zero support contacts configured. Features gated behind "add a sponsor first" will exclude users like Jasmine who aren't there yet.

## Persona: "Addict Diego" — Spanish-Speaking

### Demographics

- Age: 38
- Occupation: Construction foreman
- Location: Houston, TX (originally from Monterrey, Mexico; moved to the US 12 years ago)
- Marital Status: Married to Lucia, 3 children (ages 6, 9, and 12)
- Faith: Catholic, attends Mass weekly, active in his parish's men's group
- Recovery Stage: Early recovery (30 days sober)
- Language: Spanish-dominant bilingual — speaks English at work but thinks, prays, and processes emotions in Spanish
- Living Situation: Family home in a predominantly Hispanic neighborhood

### Background

Diego has struggled with pornography and visits to massage parlors for 10 years. His wife Lucia discovered his behavior 2 months ago when she found charges on their credit card. The revelation has devastated their marriage and shaken their family. Diego is deeply ashamed — in his culture and community, a man's sexual behavior is rarely discussed, and addiction is seen as a lack of willpower or faith rather than a treatable condition.

Diego confessed to his parish priest, who was compassionate but didn't know how to help beyond prayer and confession. The priest referred him to a Catholic counseling center, where he's been seeing a bilingual therapist (Dr. Reyes) bi-weekly. Diego also found a Spanish-language SA meeting that meets weekly in a church basement 30 minutes from his home — one of only two Spanish-language SA meetings in the greater Houston area.

Diego's English is functional for work but not for emotional processing. When he tried an English-language recovery app, he understood the words but couldn't connect with them — reading affirmations in English felt like reading a manual, not hearing God speak to him. He needs recovery tools in Spanish — not just translated Spanish, but culturally resonant Spanish that reflects his Catholic faith, his Mexican cultural context, and the way he actually thinks and prays.

### Goals

- Get sober and stay sober — for his children, his marriage, and his relationship with God
- Find recovery resources in Spanish that don't feel like afterthoughts or machine translations
- Rebuild trust with Lucia — she's considering separation and he's terrified of losing his family
- Process shame in a culturally safe context — machismo culture makes admitting sexual weakness feel impossible
- Connect with other Spanish-speaking men in recovery — he feels isolated even in recovery spaces because of the language barrier
- Integrate his Catholic faith into recovery — he needs sacramental language (confession, reconciliation, grace, redemption) alongside 12-step language

### Pain Points

- **Language as a Barrier to Healing:** English-language recovery content doesn't reach him emotionally. He can read it, but he can't feel it. Affirmations, prayers, and journal prompts need to be in the Spanish he uses to talk to God and to himself — not textbook Spanish, but the Spanish of his heart.
- **Cultural Stigma (Machismo):** In his cultural context, admitting sexual addiction is seen as admitting weakness, deviance, or moral failure. The concept of addiction as a disease is less culturally embedded than in English-speaking recovery spaces. He needs content that bridges this gap without dismissing his cultural values.
- **Catholic-Specific Faith Integration:** Most Christian recovery apps lean evangelical/Protestant. Diego needs content that resonates with Catholic spirituality — the sacraments, the saints, the Rosary, confession and reconciliation, Marian devotion, Catholic moral theology around sexuality. "Non-denominational Christian" content often feels foreign to him.
- **Scarcity of Spanish-Language Recovery Community:** Only two Spanish-language SA meetings in his metro area. Online Spanish-language recovery communities are sparse. He needs in-app community features that connect him to other Spanish-speaking men in recovery.
- **Family Financial Pressure:** His therapy costs are straining the family budget. Premium app features need to justify their cost against competing financial priorities (kids' activities, mortgage, Lucia's own counseling).
- **Work Schedule Constraints:** 10-12 hour days on construction sites with early mornings. His recovery window is narrow — early morning before work and a brief window after the kids go to bed. The app needs to fit into a physically demanding, time-constrained life.
- **Digital Literacy Gap:** Comfortable with WhatsApp, YouTube, and basic smartphone functions but not a power user. Complex app navigation or features buried behind multiple taps will go unused.
- **Spousal Relationship in Crisis:** Lucia is hurt, angry, and considering leaving. She doesn't want to use an app — she wants him to prove himself through actions. He needs tools that help him demonstrate change to her without requiring her to adopt the app herself.

### Technology Behaviors

- Uses Samsung Galaxy A14 (budget Android)
- Primary apps: WhatsApp (family and friends), YouTube (music, sermons), Facebook
- Prefers voice input over typing — especially for journaling and notes (typing in Spanish with autocorrect issues is frustrating)
- Watches content in Spanish on YouTube (sermons, music, soccer)
- Low tolerance for complexity — if he can't figure out a feature in 30 seconds, he'll skip it
- Data plan conscious — avoids features that consume large amounts of data (video streaming, large downloads)

### Quote

*"Cuando intento leer las afirmaciones en inglés, las entiendo pero no las siento. Necesito escuchar la verdad de Dios en mi idioma — en el idioma en que rezo, en que lloro, en que le pido perdón."*

Translation: *"When I try to read the affirmations in English, I understand them but I don't feel them. I need to hear God's truth in my language — the language I pray in, I cry in, I ask for forgiveness in."*

### App Design Implications

- **Spanish as a first-class experience, not a translation layer:** The Spanish version of the app must feel native — not like English content run through Google Translate. This means:
  - Culturally adapted affirmations and prayers, not literal translations (e.g., "Soy una nueva criatura en Cristo" is correct but "Hoy camino en la libertad que Cristo me ha dado" resonates more deeply in Mexican Catholic Spanish)
  - Scripture in established Spanish Bible translations (RVR1960 for Protestant-leaning content, Biblia Latinoamericana or Biblia de Jerusalén for Catholic-leaning content — user selectable)
  - Recovery terminology reviewed by Spanish-speaking counselors and recovery professionals
  - All UI elements, error messages, notifications, and system text fully translated — no English fallback in mid-flow

- **Catholic content track:** Devotionals, prayers, and spiritual content should include Catholic-specific options: prayers to the saints, Rosary-based meditations, Ignatian Examen as a check-in framework, confession preparation tools, Act of Contrition, and references to Catholic moral theology on sexuality (Theology of the Body, etc.). This content can coexist with Protestant content — the user selects their faith tradition during deferred profile setup and the app surfaces appropriate content.

- **Voice-first input:** Voice-to-text must work well in Spanish for journaling, urge logging, notes, and check-ins. This is not optional for Diego — it's his primary input method. Test with Mexican Spanish accents specifically, not just Castilian Spanish.

- **Simple, clear navigation:** Features should be discoverable in 1-2 taps. No deep menu hierarchies. Icons with text labels (not icons alone). Large tap targets. Diego's phone has a smaller screen and he may be wearing work gloves or have calloused hands — touch precision matters.

- **Low data consumption mode:** Option to download content (devotionals, affirmations, prayers) for offline use over WiFi, then access throughout the day without consuming mobile data. Video content should show estimated data usage before streaming and offer audio-only alternatives.

- **WhatsApp integration consideration:** Diego's primary communication channel is WhatsApp, not SMS or in-app messaging. If the app sends accountability broadcasts or support network notifications, offering a WhatsApp deep link alongside SMS and in-app options would dramatically increase engagement for Spanish-speaking users.

- **Community matching by language:** Community features and accountability partner matching should allow filtering by language preference. Diego needs to connect with other Spanish-speaking men in recovery — being matched with English-speaking users creates a barrier to vulnerability.

- **Spousal features that work without the spouse using the app:** Lucia may never download the app. Features like the Spouse Check-in Preparation (FANOS/FITNAP) should function as a personal preparation tool even if the spouse isn't on the platform. Progress sharing options (PDF export, screenshot-friendly milestone cards) let Diego show Lucia his progress without requiring her to engage with the technology.
