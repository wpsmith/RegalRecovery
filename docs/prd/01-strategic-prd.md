## 1. Executive Summary/Purpose

### 1.1 Why Are We Building This?

**The Problem:**
Sexual addiction, often co-occurring with multiple other addictive behaviors, affects millions of individuals and their families. Current recovery solutions are fragmented—requiring users to juggle multiple apps, journals, meeting schedules, and support networks. Those seeking recovery from a Christian perspective face an additional challenge: most secular recovery apps lack spiritual integration, while faith-based resources often lack the sophisticated digital tools necessary for daily accountability and progress tracking.

**The Opportunity:**
We are building Regal Recovery to provide a comprehensive, integrated recovery platform that addresses the whole person—mentally, intellectually, physically, emotionally, and spiritually—from a Christian worldview. This app will serve as a "digital recovery companion" that combines evidence-based addiction recovery methodologies with Christian discipleship, community accountability, and intelligent tracking.

**Business Objectives:**
- Establish market leadership in faith-based addiction recovery technology
- Achieve 50,000 active users
- Create sustainable revenue through freemium model with premium content partnerships
- Build a supportive community that reduces isolation and increases recovery success rates
- Develop partnerships with Christian counseling organizations and recovery ministries

**Success Definition:**
Users will experience increased sobriety duration, reduced relapse frequency, improved mental health metrics, and strengthened spiritual practices, supported by measurable engagement with daily tools and community features.

## 2. Target Audience

### 2.1 Primary Audience

**Core User Profile:**
- **Demographics:** Males and females, ages 18-65, predominantly 25-45
- **Geographic:** English and Spanish-speaking regions (US, Canada, UK, Australia, Mexico, and Latin America initially)
- **Faith Background:** Christians (all denominations) seeking faith-integrated recovery
- **Addiction Profile:** Primary sex addiction with common co-addictions (pornography, substance use, gambling, shopping, eating disorders)
- **Recovery Stage:** All stages—from pre-contemplation to long-term maintenance
- **Tech Proficiency:** Moderate to high; comfortable with smartphone apps and digital tools
- **Economic Status:** Middle income; willing to pay for premium resources that support recovery

### 2.2 Secondary Audiences

1. **Spouses/Partners:** Individuals supporting someone in recovery, needing their own tools and education
2. **Sponsors/Accountability Partners:** Mentors providing guidance and oversight
3. **Therapists/Counselors:** Licensed professionals managing client recovery through the platform
4. **Recovery Coaches:** Certified coaches coordinating recovery activities
5. **Ministry Leaders:** Church and para-church organization staff facilitating group recovery programs

### 2.3 User Segmentation

- **Segment A - Early Recovery (0-90 days):** High risk, needs intensive daily tools and accountability
- **Segment B - Active Recovery (90 days - 2 years):** Building habits, needs structure and milestone tracking
- **Segment C - Maintenance (2+ years):** Sustained sobriety, needs community and ongoing growth tools
- **Segment D - Multi-Addiction:** Complex recovery needs requiring multiple tracking systems
- **Segment E - Couples in Recovery:** Both individual and relational healing needs

[UPDATED — note that CSBD is classified as an impulse control disorder, NOT as an addiction; the WHO took a "relatively conservative position" due to insufficient evidence that CSBD mechanisms parallel substance use disorders. The DSM-5 does not include CSBD.] This is the first formal international diagnostic recognition of the condition, legitimizing treatment and research. However, **the U.S. still uses ICD-10** (adopted only in 2015, 23 years after publication), and ICD-11 adoption may not occur until **2030–2032**. The DSM-5 does not recognize CSBD — "Hypersexual Disorder" was proposed in 2010 but rejected by the APA. This means U.S. insurance coverage for sex addiction treatment remains limited; therapists bill under adjacent diagnoses (anxiety, OCD, impulse control). Medicare's introduction of **3 new payment codes for digital mental health devices in 2025** could eventually create a reimbursement pathway if products achieve clinical validation.

On the legislative front, **22+ U.S. states now have age-verification laws for pornography websites**, with the UK's Online Safety Act introducing similar requirements. These laws signal growing public concern and political willingness to address pornography's impacts, which drives awareness and help-seeking behavior — a tailwind for recovery products.

**Cultural trends strongly favor this market's growth.** The 2024 Barna/Pure Desire "Beyond the Porn Phenomenon" study revealed that **61% of Americans view pornography at least occasionally** (up from 55% in 2015), with **64% of men and 44% of women** reporting use. Among Gen Z, **77% of internet users watch porn** and **39% do so daily or weekly**. Yet paradoxically, Gen Z is also **the generation least comfortable with their pornography use** and most likely to seek resources — 77% want more resources to navigate sex and intimacy healthily. This creates a generational cohort that is simultaneously high-consumption and high-awareness, representing an expanding addressable market.

The Christian-specific data is particularly striking for Regal Recovery's positioning. **75% of Christian men and 40% of Christian women** report some pornography consumption. **67% of pastors** have a personal history of porn use, with **26% of pastors under 45** currently struggling. Yet **84% of U.S. adult porn users say no one is helping them** (or **74% of practicing Christians** specifically) [CORRECTED — previously cited as 82%, which is a misquotation from the Barna study] and only **10% of churches offer programming** to address it. **81% of pastors** acknowledge their churches are not adequately teaching about compulsive sexual behavior. This represents a massive unmet need within an identifiable, reachable community.

**Seasonality is predictable and actionable.** January sees the sharpest annual spike in recovery-intent searches (50–100% above baseline) driven by New Year's resolutions. November produces a secondary spike via the NoFap community's "No Nut November" challenge. The Lenten season (February–April) drives engagement in faith-based recovery as Christians commit to behavioral change. September marks fall church programming launches when recovery small groups begin. **Optimal launch timing would be early January or early September** to capture these natural demand peaks.

### 12.3 Feature and positioning gap analysis

### The market's most significant unmet needs

**No single product combines accountability, recovery, community, clinical tools, and faith integration.** The most glaring gap in the market is the absence of a comprehensive, all-in-one platform. Today, a Christian man seeking digital recovery support must typically cobble together **3–4 separate products**: Covenant Eyes for monitoring ($18/month), a recovery app like Fortify or Freedom Fight (free–$10/month), BetterHelp or a CSAT for therapy ($65–$200/week), and a separate community platform (Reddit, Celebrate Recovery). Regal Recovery's combination of tracking, journaling, FASTER Scale, PCI, community, spouse tools, 12-step integration, AI chatbot, and accountability features in one platform would be genuinely unprecedented.

**Couples and spouse-focused recovery is deeply underserved.** Bloom for Women addresses betrayal trauma for partners, and its sister product Path for Men exists for addicts — but they are separate platforms. No product unifies the addict's recovery journey with the partner's healing in a single ecosystem. Regal Recovery's spouse tools represent a significant competitive advantage, as the couple's dynamic is central to recovery success and represents a natural "two subscriptions from one household" revenue opportunity.

**Female sex addiction is almost entirely invisible in the product landscape.** Despite Barna data showing **44% of women** report pornography consumption and clinical studies finding 1.2–7.9% CSBD prevalence among women, no dedicated app targets female sex addiction. Relay serves both genders with gender-separated groups, and the Freedom Fight mentions women, but the framing, content, and marketing across the entire landscape is overwhelmingly male-focused. This is a wide-open positioning opportunity.

**The faith-based digital recovery space has only two serious players.** Covenant Eyes (monitoring, not recovery) and The Freedom Fight (comprehensive but technically fragile) are the only explicitly Christian apps with meaningful traction. Relay offers an optional Christian track, and REMOJO has Christian and Muslim content tracks, but neither leads with faith. Given that **54% of practicing Christians** report pornography use and **74% of practicing Christians say no one is helping them** [CORRECTED — previously cited as 82%], the demand vastly exceeds the supply of faith-integrated digital solutions.

**Multi-addiction tracking barely exists for behavioral addictions.** I Am Sober (11M+ downloads) proves massive demand for multi-addiction tracking but is entirely generic — not designed for the specific patterns, triggers, and recovery frameworks relevant to sex, pornography, gambling, or substance addictions. No product offers unified tracking across co-occurring behavioral addictions with specialized content for each.

**Clinical tools are absent from consumer apps.** The FASTER Scale (a relapse-prevention assessment developed by Michael Dye) and the Post-Coital Inventory (PCI) are used in clinical settings but not available in any consumer recovery app. Integrating clinically validated assessment tools would differentiate Regal Recovery from both the "tracking app" category (I Am Sober, Nomo) and the "education app" category (Fortify, Brainbuddy).

**Spanish-language sex addiction recovery tools do not exist digitally.** SAA publishes a single Spanish-language pamphlet. BlockerX supports 25+ languages for its interface. But no dedicated Spanish-language recovery program, community, or content library exists in any app. Given ~41M native Spanish speakers in the U.S. and the large Latin American market, this is a significant expansion opportunity.

**The accountability-plus-recovery integration gap is the competitive moat.** Most products are either accountability/blocking tools (Covenant Eyes, Ever Accountable, Canopy, BlockerX) OR recovery/education tools (Fortify, Brainbuddy, Freedom Fight) — rarely both. X3watch 2.0 attempted this combination but has limited traction. **Regal Recovery can own the "both" position**, particularly within the Christian market where Covenant Eyes users already demonstrate $18+/month willingness to pay for accountability alone.

### 12.4 Pricing strategy recommendation

### Recommended model: freemium with tiered subscriptions and content unlocks

The optimal pricing architecture for Regal Recovery is a **generous free tier** (to overcome stigma-driven adoption barriers) with a **tiered subscription** that escalates value through deeper recovery tools, community access, and clinical features. A hybrid approach incorporating one-time "unlocked forever" content packs alongside subscriptions can serve both price-sensitive users and those who prefer ownership over rental.

**Recommended price points:**

| Tier | Monthly | Annual | What's Included |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Free** | $0 | $0 | Basic sobriety tracker (1 addiction), daily devotional, limited journaling (7-day history), community read access, emergency prayer button, app tour |
| **Premium** | $9.99 | $79.99 | Unlimited addiction tracking, full journaling with history, FASTER Scale, progress analytics, community participation, basic AI chatbot, daily check-ins |
| **Premium+** | $14.99 | $119.99 | Everything in Premium + spouse/partner tools, PCI assessment, 12-step workbook integration, premium AI chatbot, group accountability features, all educational content |
| **Content packs** (one-time) | $19.99–$39.99 each | — | Specific curricula ("Freedom from Pornography," "Healing Your Marriage," "Understanding Your Triggers") unlocked forever |

**Rationale for these price points:** The $9.99/month Premium tier matches Fortify's premium pricing and sits at the **psychological sweet spot** below Covenant Eyes ($18/month) while signaling quality above budget trackers like I Am Sober ($3.33/month annualized). The $14.99 Premium+ tier competes directly with Ever Accountable ($14.99/month) and positions below Covenant Eyes, offering dramatically more recovery value. Annual pricing at **33% discount** to monthly incentivizes commitment (annual subscribers show **50–60% 12-month retention** vs. 20–40% for monthly).

**The one-time content pack model deserves a role but should not replace subscriptions.** One-time purchases create immediate revenue and appeal to users resistant to recurring charges. However, subscription revenue is more predictable, supports ongoing content development, and creates the "data lock-in" effect (users don't want to lose their journal history, streak data, and community relationships). **Recommendation: make the subscription the primary model, with 2–3 premium content packs available as standalone purchases that are also included free for Premium+ subscribers.** This "included with premium, available separately" hybrid maximizes both recurring revenue and impulse purchases.

### Onboarding and conversion funnel

The sensitivity of this topic demands an **unusually patient conversion funnel**. Standard health-app tactics (aggressive Day 0 paywall, countdown timers, inflated diagnostic scores) would be counterproductive and ethically inappropriate for this audience. QUITTR's aggressive approach has generated significant user backlash and should serve as an anti-pattern.

**Recommended approach:**

- **Day 0–7: Free value and trust-building.** Users access the free tier with no paywall prompts. The app demonstrates value through daily devotionals, a sobriety tracker, and the emergency prayer button. An optional 3-minute assessment (non-alarmist, validated) helps personalize the experience.
- **Day 7–14: Soft upgrade prompts.** After a week of engagement, introduce "Premium tier preview" moments — when users attempt to access deeper journaling, the FASTER Scale, or community posting, show what they'd unlock. No hard paywalls.
- **Day 14: Offer a 14-day free trial** of Premium tier (not Premium+). Industry data shows **longer trials (17–32 days) achieve ~48.8% conversion** vs. ~25% for standard 7-day trials. The 14-day trial gives users time to experience meaningful progress.
- **Day 28+: Conversion.** By now, users have a 28-day streak, journal entries, and community connections they don't want to lose. Convert with emphasis on protecting their progress data.

**Critical design principle:** 80–90% of trial decisions happen on Day 0. The onboarding experience must immediately communicate safety, privacy, and non-judgment. Use an **anonymous app icon** (like SAA Toolkit's approach) and a **generic name on the home screen** (perhaps just "RR" or a cross icon).

### Retention levers specific to recovery

Recovery apps have a unique retention advantage: **the user's sobriety data is inherently sticky.** Unlike a meditation app where sessions are fungible, a recovery app accumulates irreplaceable personal data — streak counts, journal entries, FASTER Scale assessments over time, community relationships, and 12-step progress. Specific retention levers include:

- **Streak psychology:** The loss aversion of breaking a streak is one of the most powerful retention mechanisms in consumer apps. Recovery streaks should be prominently displayed and celebrated at milestones (7, 30, 90, 180, 365 days) with shareable (anonymous) milestone cards.
- **Accountability partner dependency:** Once a user has established accountability relationships within the app, switching costs rise dramatically. The social graph becomes a moat.
- **Progressive content unlocking:** Structure the 12-step workbook and educational curricula as sequential unlocks tied to recovery milestones. Users who've completed Step 4 within the app won't restart elsewhere.
- **Community investment:** As users build reputation and relationships in recovery groups, the community itself becomes the product. Relay's 4.8-star rating is driven primarily by community quality.
- **Clinical data continuity:** FASTER Scale assessments and PCI results create a longitudinal clinical record that users (and their therapists) value. Offer data export as a retention hedge — users appreciate the option but rarely use it.
- **Spouse/partner integration:** When both partners use the platform, churn from either is dramatically reduced. The couple's shared recovery data creates bilateral lock-in.

### Pricing pitfalls unique to this space

**Payment privacy is a critical concern but less problematic than assumed.** Apple App Store charges appear on statements as "APPLE.COM/BILL" without revealing the specific app. Google Play shows "GOOGLE*[Developer Name]." **Recommendation: Use a neutral developer/company name** like "Regal Digital" or "RR Health" for billing purposes. Never use "sex addiction" in any billing-visible string.

**HSA/FSA eligibility is achievable and represents a competitive advantage.** Mental health treatment for diagnosed conditions qualifies for HSA/FSA reimbursement. While sex addiction isn't a standalone DSM-5 diagnosis, CSBD is in ICD-11, and related conditions (anxiety, depression, impulse control disorders) can justify medical necessity through a Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN) from a licensed provider. Headspace has pioneered LMN generation at checkout. **Recommendation: Build LMN generation into the Premium+ tier** and partner with licensed providers who can issue them. This could reduce effective cost by 20–35% for users with HSA/FSA accounts.

**Price sensitivity is real but paradoxical.** Many sex addicts face financial distress from their addiction (job loss, divorce costs, legal fees), reducing ability to pay. Yet the urgency of their crisis increases willingness to invest. Free alternatives abound (SAA, Celebrate Recovery, Setting Captives Free, re:generation) — creating a strong zero-price anchor. The generous free tier addresses this by ensuring no one is excluded from basic tools due to cost, while premium features justify their price through clinical and community value that free alternatives cannot match.

**Tithing culture may compress discretionary budgets** in the Christian market. Many Christian families budget 10% of income to church giving, with additional amounts for Christian media, education, and resources. Positioning Regal Recovery as a **ministry tool** (alongside, not competing with, tithing) and offering church-subsidized access can mitigate this.

**Economics of "unlocked forever" vs. subscription:**

The "unlocked forever" model is intentional and strategically sound for this market, despite the typical SaaS preference for recurring revenue. Here's why:

1. **Trust economics.** This audience has been burned — by their addiction, by relationships, and often by aggressive monetization (see QUITTR). 2. **Content packs supplement, not replace, subscriptions.** The core value of Premium/Premium+ is the living, data-driven features — tracking, FASTER Scale, AI agent, community, therapist portal, content filter. Content packs are static assets (devotionals, affirmations, prayer collections). A user who buys a $19.99 devotional pack but doesn't subscribe still has no access to journaling history, clinical assessments, or the AI agent. The pack creates a taste; the subscription delivers the meal.

3. **Revenue modeling shows packs as upside, not cannibalization.**

| Scenario | Pack buyers who never subscribe | Pack buyers who later subscribe | Net effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conservative | 70% | 30% | Packs generate $4–$20 from users who'd otherwise pay $0 |
| Moderate | 50% | 50% | Packs serve as conversion funnel; 50% become subscribers |
| Optimistic | 30% | 70% | Packs act as "try before you commit" stepping stone |

Even in the conservative case, packs monetize users who resist subscriptions — incremental revenue, not cannibalized revenue. The "included free in Premium+" incentivizes subscription upgrades: "Why buy packs individually when Premium+ includes them all?"

4. **Marcus's reality.** Persona 3.9 (Marcus, union apprentice, Charlotte, NC) earns ~$35–$45K/year. At $9.99/month, Premium is $120/year — meaningful but manageable at ~0.3% of gross income. But the free trial + content pack path gives Marcus a lower-commitment entry: try free for 7 days, buy a $4.99 affirmations pack he loves, then subscribe to Premium 3 months later when he's committed to recovery. The content pack is the on-ramp that a $9.99/month paywall would block.

### Insurance, HSA/FSA, and EAP considerations

**HSA/FSA eligibility should be pursued immediately.** It requires partnering with licensed providers who can generate Letters of Medical Necessity, not FDA clearance or insurance contracts. This is achievable within months, not years, and provides a tangible value advantage over every competitor except BetterHelp. Display "HSA/FSA eligible with provider letter" prominently on the pricing page.

**Insurance reimbursement is a long-term play (3–5+ years).** Until the U.S. adopts ICD-11 (estimated 2030–2032) and CSBD receives broader diagnostic acceptance, direct insurance billing is impractical. However, building superbill generation into the app (allowing users to submit to insurance for partial out-of-network reimbursement under adjacent diagnoses) is low-effort and immediately valuable.

**EAP partnerships** represent an untapped corporate channel. **74% of U.S. employers** offer mental health apps through EAPs or corporate wellness programs. Calm Business charges ~$64/employee/year for organizational access. A recovery-focused app could partner with EAP providers as a specialized referral resource, though the stigma barrier for employer-provided sex addiction tools is significant. Position as "behavioral health and accountability" rather than "sex addiction" for corporate channels.

### App Store optimization for a sensitive category

- **App name:** Use "Regal Recovery" without "sex addiction" in the title. Include relevant keywords in subtitle/short description: "Faith-Based Recovery & Accountability"
